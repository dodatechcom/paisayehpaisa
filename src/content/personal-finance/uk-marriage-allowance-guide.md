Marriage Allowance is a simple tax relief that lets a lower-earning spouse or civil partner transfer part of their personal allowance to the higher earner. It saves eligible couples up to **£252 per year** — and you can backdate claims for up to four tax years, potentially putting over £1,000 back in your pocket.

Despite being available since 2015, millions of eligible couples have not claimed. If you or your partner earns below the personal allowance, it is worth checking whether you qualify.

## What Is Marriage Allowance?

Marriage Allowance allows the lower earner in a marriage or civil partnership to transfer **£1,260** of their personal allowance to their partner. The higher earner then pays less income tax.

For the 2025/26 tax year, the personal allowance is **£12,570**. If one spouse earns less than this amount, they are not using their full personal allowance. Marriage Allowance lets them pass £1,260 of that unused allowance to their partner — reducing the higher earner's tax bill by up to **£252 per year**.

## Who Is Eligible?

You must meet all of the following conditions:

- **One spouse earns below the personal allowance** — less than £12,570 per year
- **The other spouse earns between £12,571 and £50,270** — the basic rate tax band
- **Both spouses were born after 6 April 1935**
- **You are married or in a civil partnership**

The scheme does not apply to cohabiting couples. You must be legally married or in a civil partnership.

If both partners earn above the personal allowance, Marriage Allowance does not apply. The saving only works when one partner has unused personal allowance to transfer.

## How to Claim

Claiming takes about 5 minutes online:

1. Go to [GOV.UK — Marriage Allowance](https://www.gov.uk/marriage-allowance)
2. Sign in with your Government Gateway account
3. Enter both National Insurance numbers
4. Confirm the lower earner's income
5. HMRC processes the claim and adjusts the higher earner's tax code

Both spouses must agree to the transfer. The lower earner is the one who makes the claim and authorises the transfer.

You can also claim by calling HMRC on 0300 200 3300 if you prefer not to claim online.

## Backdated Claims

You can backdate your claim for the **previous four tax years**. This means if you have been eligible but not claimed, you could receive a lump sum.

| Tax Year | Maximum Saving |
|----------|---------------|
| 2025/26 | £252 |
| 2024/25 | £252 |
| 2023/24 | £252 |
| 2022/23 | £252 |
| **Total (4 years)** | **£1,008** |

The personal allowance has been £12,570 since 2021/22, so the annual saving has been £252 for each of the last four tax years. If you have been eligible throughout this period and not claimed, you can receive all four years' worth of savings in a single claim.

## How It Works

The mechanics are straightforward:

1. The lower earner gives up **£1,260** of their personal allowance
2. The higher earner's personal allowance increases by **£1,260**
3. The tax saving equals **£1,260 x 20%** (the basic rate) = **£252 per year**

For example, if the higher earner's personal allowance is normally £12,570, it rises to **£13,830** with Marriage Allowance. They pay no income tax on the first £13,830 of their earnings.

## Worked Example

Sarah earns £10,000 per year. James earns £35,000 per year.

- Sarah's personal allowance: £12,570
- Sarah's unused allowance: £2,570 (she earns £2,570 below the threshold)
- Sarah transfers £1,260 to James
- James's new personal allowance: £13,830
- James saves: £1,260 x 20% = **£252 per year**

Sarah continues to pay no income tax on her £10,000 earnings, since her remaining personal allowance of £11,310 still covers her income.

The couple's combined tax bill falls by £252 each year — or up to £1,008 if they backdate four years.

## Marriage Allowance vs Other Tax Reliefs

Marriage Allowance is one of several tax reliefs available to UK households. It is worth understanding how it fits alongside others:

| Relief | Annual Benefit | Who Qualifies |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Marriage Allowance | Up to £252 | Couples where one earns below £12,570 |
| Personal Allowance | £12,570 tax-free | Everyone |
| Blind Person's Allowance | £2,870 extra | Registered blind or partially sighted |
| Gift Aid | 25p extra per £1 donated | Basic rate taxpayers |

Marriage Allowance is unique because it is the only relief that lets one person transfer part of their tax-free allowance to another.

## Tips for Maximising Your Savings

- **Check if you are eligible** — even a small saving adds up over time, especially with backdated claims
- **Claim even if the saving seems small** — £252 per year is £21 per month, which over four years becomes £1,008
- **Backdate up to four years** — do not leave money on the table if you have been eligible but not claimed
- **Both spouses must agree** — the lower earner must authorise the transfer, and both must be happy to proceed
- **Cancel if circumstances change** — if you separate, divorce or your incomes change so that you no longer qualify, cancel the claim to avoid overpayments
- **Reclaim if you forgot to claim previously** — HMRC will pay backdated amounts directly

## What Happens if Your Circumstances Change?

Marriage Allowance is not a permanent arrangement. If your circumstances change — for example, the lower earner starts earning above the personal allowance, or you separate — you should cancel the claim.

You can cancel online at GOV.UK or by calling HMRC. HMRC will adjust your tax code accordingly. If you have received a payment you were not entitled to, HMRC may ask you to repay it.

## Can You Claim if One Spouse Is Self-Employed?

Yes. Marriage Allowance applies regardless of whether you are employed, self-employed or a combination of both. The key requirement is that one spouse earns below the personal allowance and the other earns within the basic rate band (up to £50,270).

If one spouse is self-employed and the other is employed, the self-employed spouse can still transfer their unused personal allowance.

Marriage Allowance is one of the easiest tax reliefs to claim in the UK. If you or your partner earns below the personal allowance, it is worth checking your eligibility on GOV.UK today.
