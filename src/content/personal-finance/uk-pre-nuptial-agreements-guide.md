## What Is a Pre-Nuptial Agreement?

A pre-nuptial agreement (commonly called a pre-nup) is a legal document created by a couple before they marry or enter a civil partnership. It sets out how assets, property, and finances would be divided if the relationship ends in divorce or dissolution.

Pre-nuptial agreements are not automatically legally binding in England and Wales. However, the courts will give them significant weight if certain conditions are met. Since the landmark case of *Radmacher v Granatino* in 2010, the Supreme Court has confirmed that pre-nups should be respected provided both parties entered into them freely and with full understanding.

## Legal Status in the UK

In England and Wales, pre-nuptial agreements are not legally binding in the same way as a contract. A court retains the final say on financial arrangements in divorce proceedings. However, the court will generally follow a pre-nup if:

- Both parties had **full and frank disclosure** of their financial circumstances
- Both parties took **independent legal advice** before signing
- There was **no undue pressure** or duress on either party
- The agreement was signed at least **28 days before the wedding**
- The agreement is **fair** and does not prejudice the needs of any children

The court's primary consideration is always the welfare of any children involved. If a pre-nup would leave a child in a disadvantageous position, the court may override its terms.

## What Can a Pre-Nup Cover?

A pre-nuptial agreement can address a wide range of financial matters:

- **Property** — the family home, second homes, and buy-to-let investments
- **Savings** — bank accounts, ISAs, and cash savings
- **Investments** — stocks, shares, investment funds, and pensions
- **Business interests** — ownership stakes in companies and partnerships
- **Inheritances** — assets received or expected to be received
- **Debts** — liabilities each party brings into the marriage
- **Future income** — in some cases, agreements can address how future earnings are treated

A pre-nup **cannot** make arrangements for child custody or contact. Those decisions are always made by the court based on the child's best interests. It also cannot seek to limitChild Maintenance Service obligations.

## How to Create a Pre-Nuptial Agreement

Both parties must follow a structured process to create a pre-nup that a court is likely to uphold:

1. **Both parties need independent solicitors** — you cannot use the same lawyer. Each person must receive advice from their own legal representative.
2. **Full financial disclosure** — both parties must provide complete and honest details of their assets, income, and debts.
3. **No pressure** — the agreement must be entered into freely. One party cannot pressure or coerce the other into signing.
4. **Signed at least 28 days before the wedding** — last-minute agreements are less likely to be upheld.
5. **Written and signed** — the agreement should be in writing and signed by both parties in the presence of a witness.

The process typically takes several months, so start well before your wedding date.

## How Much Does It Cost?

Pre-nuptial agreements in the UK typically cost between £1,000 and £3,000 per person, depending on:

- **Complexity of assets** — straightforward agreements are cheaper; complex financial arrangements cost more
- **Whether the couple is amicable** — cooperative couples can reduce costs by agreeing terms before instructing solicitors
- **Solicitor fees** — rates vary between firms and regions

If both parties are in agreement and the financial situation is simple, costs may be at the lower end. Complex situations involving business assets, multiple properties, or international considerations will push costs higher.

## When to Consider a Pre-Nup

Pre-nuptial agreements are not just for the wealthy. They can be valuable in several situations:

- **Second marriages** — protecting assets from a previous relationship or children from an earlier partnership
- **Children from a previous relationship** — ensuring assets pass to your biological children
- **Business owners** — protecting a company or partnership from being treated as a marital asset
- **Significant wealth disparity** — where one party has substantially more assets or income
- **Inheritance expectations** — if one party expects a significant inheritance
- **One party has significant debts** — protecting the other party from liability for those debts
- **Family pressure** — where one family wants to protect assets from potential division

Even if you do not think you need one, a pre-nup can provide peace of mind and reduce conflict if the relationship ends.

## Worked Example

Sarah owns a house worth £400,000, has £50,000 in savings, and expects a future inheritance. Tom has no property, £20,000 in savings, and carries £10,000 in student debt.

They create a pre-nup that states:

- Sarah's house remains her property if they divorce
- Each party keeps their own savings
- Tom's student debt remains his responsibility
- If they divorce after 10 years of marriage and have children, Tom receives 25% of the increase in the house's value during the marriage

This protects Sarah's pre-marital assets while providing fairness if the marriage is long-term and children are involved.

## Tips for a Fair Pre-Nup

- **Both get independent lawyers** — never use the same solicitor. Each party needs their own advice.
- **Full disclosure** — hide nothing. Incomplete disclosure can invalidate the agreement.
- **Sign early** — sign at least 28 days before the wedding. Earlier is better.
- **Review regularly** — review the agreement every 5 years or after major life events (children, inheritance, job change).
- **Don't use the same solicitor** — this is both unethical and undermines the agreement's validity.
- **Consider children's needs** — the court will prioritise children. Ensure the agreement does not disadvantage them.
- **Be realistic** — a court may overturn an agreement that is blatantly unfair to one party.
- **Keep it civil** — approach the process as a mutual protection measure, not a sign of distrust.

## Common Misconceptions

- **"Pre-nups are only for rich people"** — anyone with assets, debts, or children from a previous relationship can benefit
- **"Pre-nups are unromantic"** — many couples see them as practical financial planning
- **"A pre-nup means the divorce is easier"** — it reduces financial disputes but does not eliminate the need for legal proceedings
- **"Pre-nups are always enforceable"** — they are influential but not binding; the court retains discretion

## What Happens Without a Pre-Nup?

If you divorce without a pre-nuptial agreement, the court will divide assets based on factors including:

- The needs of both parties
- The standard of living during the marriage
- The length of the marriage
- The contributions of each party (financial and non-financial)
- The welfare of any children

This process can be more expensive, contentious, and unpredictable than following the terms of a pre-nup.

## Alternative: Post-Nuptial Agreements

If you are already married and did not sign a pre-nup, you can create a **post-nuptial agreement** at any time during the marriage. The same legal principles apply: independent solicitors, full disclosure, and no pressure. Post-nups carry the same legal weight as pre-nups in the eyes of the court.

## Final Verdict

Pre-nuptial agreements are a practical financial planning tool for UK couples. While not automatically binding, they are highly influential in court proceedings if created properly. The process requires independent legal advice, full financial disclosure, and fair terms.

For couples with assets to protect, children from previous relationships, or significant wealth disparity, a pre-nup can save time, money, and emotional stress in the future.
