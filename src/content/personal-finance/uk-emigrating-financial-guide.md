Moving abroad is exciting. Sorting your UK finances before you leave is not. But getting it right can save you thousands in tax, avoid penalties, and make sure you do not leave money on the table. This guide covers everything you need to do with your UK finances before you emigrate.

## Statutory Residence Test (SRT)

The Statutory Residence Test determines whether you are UK tax resident for a given tax year. When you leave the UK, you need to establish non-resident status to stop being taxed on your worldwide income.

### How It Works

The SRT uses a **sufficient ties test** that considers:

- **Family tie** — Do your spouse, civil partner, or children live in the UK?
- **Accommodation tie** — Do you have accessible accommodation in the UK that you use for 91+ days in the year?
- **Work tie** — Do you work in the UK for 40+ days in a tax year?
- **90-day tie** — Did you spend 90+ days in the UK in either of the previous two tax years?
- **Country tie** — Did you spend more days in the UK than in any other single country?

### Leaving the UK

To become non-resident, you generally need to:

- Spend fewer days in the UK than in any other country
- Sever ties with the UK (family, accommodation, work)
- Establish a home in your new country

The SRT applies to each individual tax year (6 April to 5 April). You can become non-resident part-way through a tax year.

## ISA Rules

If you hold an Individual Savings Account (ISA), you must **close it before you become non-resident**. You cannot hold an ISA as a non-UK resident.

### What to Do

- Withdraw all money from your ISA — withdrawals are **tax-free**
- Close the account before 6 April following the tax year you become non-resident
- You will not lose any interest earned up to the point of closure
- You can reinvest the money in your new country

### Can You Keep It Open?

No. HMRC requires ISA providers to close accounts held by non-residents. If you do not close it voluntarily, the provider will do it for you. Either way, you get the money tax-free.

## Pensions

You can keep your UK pension when you move abroad. You do not have to transfer it.

### Keeping Your UK Pension

- Your pension stays in the UK and continues to grow tax-free
- You can draw from it once you reach your pension's normal minimum pension age
- Payments will be taxed in the UK — but you may be able to claim relief under a double taxation agreement
- Your pension provider may deduct UK tax on payments unless you apply for relief

### Transferring to QROPS

A **Qualifying Recognised Overseas Pension Scheme (QROPS)** is an overseas pension scheme that meets HMRC's requirements. You can transfer your UK pension to a QROPS, but there are considerations:

- **Transfer fee** — typically £1,000 to £5,000 depending on the scheme
- **Annual management charge** — usually 0.5% to 2% per year
- **UK tax on transfer** — an overseas transfer charge of 25% may apply if you transfer within 5 years of leaving the UK and do not meet certain conditions
- **Restricted access** — you may not be able to access your pension until age 55 (rising to 57 in 2028)

Check your pension's transfer value and the QROPS fees before deciding. Speak to a regulated pension adviser.

## Capital Gains Tax (CGT) on Departure

When you leave the UK, you face a **deemed disposal** on certain assets. This means you must pay CGT as if you had sold all your chargeable assets on the date you leave.

### What Is a Deemed Disposal?

- UK residents pay CGT on gains made when they sell assets
- On emigration, HMRC treats assets as sold at market value on your departure date
- You pay CGT on any gain between your acquisition cost and the market value at departure
- You can use your annual CGT exemption (£3,000 for 2025-26) in the year of departure

### Exceptions and Reliefs

Some assets qualify for relief:

- **Business Asset Disposal Relief (BADR)** — previously Entrepreneurs' Relief — reduces CGT to 10% on qualifying business assets up to £1 million
- **Principal Private Residence Relief** — your main home is generally exempt from CGT
- **Gifts to spouse or civil partner** — transfers between spouses are usually exempt

### Practical Steps

- Get a professional valuation of your assets before departure
- Calculate your total gains for the year of departure
- Report and pay CGT through Self Assessment by 31 January following the tax year of departure

## National Insurance Contributions

You can pay **voluntary Class 2 and Class 3 NI contributions** to protect your entitlement to the UK State Pension.

### Why Pay Voluntary NI?

- You need 35 qualifying years of NI contributions for a full State Pension
- If you leave the UK before reaching 35 years, voluntary contributions fill the gaps
- The State Pension is currently £221.20 per week (2025-26) — it is worth protecting

### How to Pay

- Contact HMRC to arrange voluntary contributions
- Class 3 voluntary NI costs approximately £17.45 per week (2025-26)
- You can pay for up to 6 years of missed contributions
- Check if your new country has a **Social Security Agreement** (totalisation agreement) with the UK — some countries credit your UK NI contributions towards their state pension

### Countries With Social Security Agreements

The UK has agreements with over 30 countries, including:

- Australia, Canada, USA, New Zealand
- Most EU countries
- Japan, South Korea, India (limited)

Check the full list on GOV.UK.

## Student Loan Repayments

If you have a UK student loan, you **must continue repading it** even if you move abroad.

### How It Works

- You must contact the **Student Loans Company (SLC)** before you leave
- SLC will set up a direct debit or fixed repayment arrangement
- Repayments are based on your overseas income, converted to pounds
- The repayment threshold is the same as for UK residents — you only repay above the threshold
- If you fail to contact SLC, they may charge a fixed monthly amount regardless of income

### What to Do

1. Notify SLC that you are going abroad
2. Provide your new address and employment details
3. Set up a direct debit for repayments
4. Keep records of all repayments made abroad

## Bank Accounts

You can generally **keep your UK bank accounts** when you move abroad, but there are some restrictions.

### What to Check

- **Residency requirements** — some banks require you to be a UK resident. They may close your account if you tell them you have moved abroad
- **Address updates** — you will need to update your address to your overseas address
- **Debit card** — cards are usually posted to your UK address. You may need a UK address to receive replacement cards
- **Online access** — most UK banks allow you to access online banking from abroad
- **Fees** — using a UK debit card abroad may incur charges

### Which Banks Allow Non-Residents?

Policies vary. Some banks that have historically been more accommodating to non-residents include:

- **HSBC** — particularly if you have a Premier account
- **Barclays** — case-by-case basis
- **Nationwide** — generally requires UK address

Contact your bank directly to ask about their non-resident policy before you leave.

## Investments

You can keep your UK investments when you move abroad, but there are reporting obligations.

### What to Do

- **Report income** — you must report UK investment income (dividends, interest) to your new country's tax authority
- **Double taxation agreements** — the UK has agreements with many countries to prevent double taxation. You may be able to claim relief in your new country for UK tax already paid
- **Capital gains** — gains made after you leave the UK are not subject to UK CGT (unless the asset is UK residential property)
- **ISAs** — you must close ISAs before departure (see above)
- **GIAs** — you can keep a General Investment Account, but income and gains are taxable

### Tax Treatment in Your New Country

The tax treatment depends on your new country of residence. Some countries tax worldwide income, others do not. Research the tax rules in your destination country or get professional advice.

## Tax on Departure: Self Assessment

You must complete a **Self Assessment tax return** for the year you leave the UK.

### What to Include

- Your worldwide income up to the date of departure
- Any UK-sourced income after departure (rental income, dividends, etc.)
- Capital gains including deemed disposal gains
- Claims for double taxation relief

### Deadline

- File online by 31 January following the tax year of departure
- Pay any tax due by 31 January

### Notify HMRC

You must also notify HMRC that you are leaving the UK. You can do this through your Self Assessment tax return or by contacting HMRC directly.

## Worked Example: Mark's Financial Checklist

Mark is a 35-year-old software developer emigrating to Australia. Here is his financial situation and the actions he needs to take.

### Mark's Assets

| Asset | Value |
|---|---|
| ISA | £50,000 |
| Workplace pension | £80,000 |
| General investment account | £30,000 |
| UK bank account | £10,000 |
| **Total** | **£170,000** |

### Actions for Mark

**ISA (£50,000)**

- Close the ISA before becoming non-resident
- Withdraw all £50,000 tax-free
- Transfer to an Australian savings account or reinvest there
- No UK or Australian tax on the withdrawal

**Pension (£80,000)**

- Keep the UK pension for now
- Do not transfer to QROPS immediately — the 25% overseas transfer charge may apply if transferred within 5 years
- Let it grow in the UK
- Consider a QROPS transfer later when the 5-year window has passed and he has settled in Australia

**General Investment Account (£30,000)**

- Keep the GIA — he can continue holding UK investments
- Report dividends and interest to the Australian Taxation Office
- Australia taxes worldwide income, but UK dividends may qualify for a franking credit
- CGT: any gains made after leaving the UK are not subject to UK CGT

**Bank Account (£10,000)**

- Keep the UK bank account — useful for UK transactions
- Check if his bank allows non-resident accounts
- Update his address to his Australian address

**National Insurance**

- Mark has 12 qualifying years of NI contributions
- He wants a full UK State Pension (35 years needed)
- He will pay voluntary Class 3 NI contributions to protect his pension
- Australia has a Social Security Agreement with the UK — his Australian contributions may count towards UK pension

**Student Loan**

- Mark does not have a student loan — no action needed

**Self Assessment**

- Mark will complete a Self Assessment return for the year of departure
- He will report his worldwide income up to the date of departure
- He will claim double taxation relief for any Australian tax on UK income

### Mark's Timeline

- **6 months before departure:** Get tax advice, check pension options, research Australian tax rules
- **3 months before:** Close ISA, notify SLC (if applicable), update bank address
- **1 month before:** Get asset valuations, prepare Self Assessment
- **On departure:** Notify HMRC, establish Australian tax residency
- **After arrival:** Register with Australian tax office, report UK income

## Top Tips for Emigrating

1. **Plan 6 months ahead** — give yourself time to sort everything
2. **Close your ISA early** — you must do this before becoming non-resident
3. **Check your pension options** — get advice on whether to keep or transfer
4. **Get tax advice** — a cross-border tax adviser can save you money
5. **Research your new country's tax rules** — every country is different
6. **Keep your UK bank account** — it makes life easier for UK transactions
7. **Pay voluntary NI** — protect your UK State Pension entitlement
8. **Keep records** — save all correspondence with HMRC, pension providers, and your bank
9. **Notify everyone** — HMRC, pension provider, bank, SLC (if applicable)
10. **Do not panic** — most of these tasks are straightforward if you plan ahead
