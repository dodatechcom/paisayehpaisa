The UK government offers generous tax reliefs to encourage individuals to invest in small, early-stage companies. Enterprise Investment Schemes (EIS), Seed Enterprise Investment Schemes (SEIS) and Venture Capital Trusts (VCTs) each provide distinct benefits — from income tax relief to capital gains exemptions and inheritance tax planning. Here is everything you need to know.

## What Is EIS (Enterprise Investment Scheme)?

EIS lets you invest in qualifying UK small companies and claim **30% income tax relief** on your investment, up to a maximum of **£1 million per tax year** (or £2 million if at least £1 million is invested in knowledge-intensive companies).

To qualify for EIS relief, the company must:

- Be UK-based and unlisted (or listed on AIM)
- Have fewer than 250 employees
- Have gross assets of no more than £15 million before the investment
- Not have been trading for more than 10 years
- Be engaged in a qualifying trade (most trades qualify, but certain excluded activities such as financial services, property development and hotel operations do not)

### EIS Tax Benefits

| Benefit | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Income tax relief** | 30% of amount invested, against your income tax liability |
| **Annual investment limit** | £1 million (£2 million for knowledge-intensive companies) |
| **Capital gains deferral** | Any capital gain can be deferred if reinvested into EIS shares |
| **Loss relief** | If shares become worthless, you can offset the loss against income or capital gains |
| **Inheritance tax relief** | Shares qualifying for EIS are exempt from IHT after being held for 2 years |
| **Holding period** | Shares must be held for at least 3 years to retain income tax relief |

The capital gains deferral is particularly powerful. If you have realised a gain on property, shares or another asset, you can defer the CGT liability by reinvesting the gain into EIS-qualifying companies. The deferred gain crystallises when you eventually sell the EIS shares.

## What Is SEIS (Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme)?

SEIS is designed for even earlier-stage companies than EIS. It offers **50% income tax relief**, making it the most generous scheme by percentage, though the investment limits are lower.

To qualify for SEIS relief, the company must:

- Have been trading for less than 3 years
- Have gross assets of no more than £250,000 before the investment
- Have fewer than 25 employees
- Not have raised more than £250,000 through SEIS previously

### SEIS Tax Benefits

| Benefit | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Income tax relief** | 50% of amount invested |
| **Annual investment limit** | £200,000 |
| **Capital gains exemption** | 50% of gains reinvested into SEIS are exempt from CGT |
| **Loss relief** | Available if shares become worthless |
| **Holding period** | Shares must be held for at least 3 years |

The risk is higher with SEIS companies — they are genuinely early-stage — but the tax relief reflects that. The 50% CGT exemption on reinvested gains is unique to SEIS and does not apply to EIS.

## What Are VCTs (Venture Capital Trusts)?

VCTs are publicly listed companies on the London Stock Exchange that invest in qualifying small companies on your behalf. Unlike EIS and SEIS where you invest directly, VCTs let you buy shares in a fund structure.

### VCT Tax Benefits

| Benefit | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Income tax relief** | 30% of amount invested, up to £200,000 per tax year |
| **Tax-free dividends** | Dividends from VCTs are free from income tax |
| **Capital gains exemption** | No CGT on disposal of VCT shares |
| **Holding period** | Shares must be held for at least 5 years to retain income tax relief |

VCTs are the easiest of the three schemes to access because they trade on the stock exchange. You can buy and sell VCT shares through a standard brokerage account, though the market is less liquid than mainstream equities.

Popular VCTs include Octopus Titan VCT, British Smaller Companies VCT and Albion VCTs. Most VCTs charge ongoing fees of 1.5–3% per year, which eat into returns.

## EIS vs SEIS vs VCT: Comparison

| Feature | EIS | SEIS | VCT |
|---------|-----|------|-----|
| **Income tax relief** | 30% | 50% | 30% |
| **Max investment per year** | £1m (£2m knowledge-intensive) | £200k | £200k |
| **CGT deferral/exemption** | Deferral | 50% exemption | Full exemption on disposal |
| **Dividends** | Taxable | Taxable | Tax-free |
| **IHT relief** | After 2 years | After 2 years | After 2 years |
| **Holding period** | 3 years | 3 years | 5 years |
| **Risk level** | High | Very high | High |
| **Liquidity** | Low (unlisted) | Low (unlisted) | Medium (listed on LSE) |
| **Management** | Direct investment | Direct investment | Managed fund |

## How to Invest

### EIS and SEIS

You invest directly in individual companies. The main platforms for EIS and SEIS investments are:

- **Seedrs** — Equity crowdfunding platform with a curated range of EIS-eligible companies
- **Crowdcube** — Similar crowdfunding model with EIS-qualifying opportunities
- **Octopus Investments** — Specialist in EIS and SEIS funds, offering diversified portfolios
- **Kin Capital** — EIS fund manager focusing on later-stage companies

For most investors, a diversified EIS or SEIS fund is preferable to picking individual companies. Funds spread your investment across multiple companies, reducing the impact of any single failure.

### VCTs

VCTs are listed on the London Stock Exchange, so you can buy them through:

- **Hargreaves Lansdown** — The UK's largest retail investment platform
- **AJ Bell** — Competitive fees and a wide range of VCTs
- **Interactive Investor** — Good for larger portfolios with a flat-fee model
- **ii or Bestinvest** — Both offer VCT access

You do not need a special account — VCTs can be held in a general investment account, ISA or SIPP, though the tax relief only applies to the VCT shares themselves, not to the wrapper.

## Risks to Understand

EIS, SEIS and VCTs are not suitable for every investor. The key risks include:

- **Illiquidity** — EIS and SEIS shares cannot be easily sold. You are typically locked in for at least 3 years. Even VCT shares, while listed, trade in a thin market with wide spreads.
- **High failure rate** — Startups and small companies fail frequently. You may lose some or all of your investment in individual companies.
- **Complex tax rules** — HMRC imposes strict conditions. If the company does not qualify, or you sell too early, you lose the tax relief and may have to pay it back.
- **Not FSCS protected** — Investments in EIS, SEIS and VCTs are not covered by the Financial Services Compensation Scheme. If the platform or fund collapses, you have no protection.
- **Reliance on tax relief** — The schemes only work if you have sufficient income tax liability to offset against. If your income drops, the relief becomes less valuable.

## Who Should Consider These Schemes?

EIS, SEIS and VCTs suit a specific type of investor:

- **Higher-rate taxpayers** — The 30% or 50% relief is most valuable to those paying 40% or 45% income tax. A 40% taxpayer investing £100,000 in EIS saves £30,000 in income tax.
- **People with capital gains to defer or exempt** — If you have sold a property, business or investment portfolio and face a large CGT bill, EIS deferral or SEIS exemption can reduce or delay the tax.
- **Experienced investors comfortable with risk** — These are not mainstream investments. You need to understand that you could lose money, even with the tax relief.
- **Angel investors** — If you want to support early-stage companies and are comfortable with direct equity investments, EIS and SEIS offer a tax-efficient way to do so.

They are **not** suitable for:

- Basic-rate taxpayers (the relief is less impactful)
- Investors who need liquidity (you cannot easily access your money)
- Anyone who cannot afford to lose the investment
- People who do not have sufficient income tax liability to use the relief

## Worked Example: Higher-Rate Taxpayer

Consider Sarah, a higher-rate taxpayer earning £100,000 per year. She has just sold an investment property and has a **£150,000 capital gain**.

She invests **£100,000** of the gain into an EIS-qualifying fund.

| Step | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Capital gain on property sale | £150,000 |
| Investment into EIS | £100,000 |
| Income tax relief (30% of £100,000) | £30,000 saved |
| CGT deferral (gain deferred until EIS shares sold) | £100,000 deferred |
| EIS shares value after 3 years | £150,000 (assuming 50% return) |
| CGT deferral crystallises | £100,000 gain becomes taxable |
| Net benefit | £30,000 tax relief + £50,000 growth on EIS shares |

Sarah has saved £30,000 in income tax immediately, deferred £100,000 of capital gains, and her EIS investment has grown by £50,000. The deferred gain will eventually be taxed, but she has had use of the money for 3 years and earned a strong return in the meantime.

## Tips for Getting Started

- **Diversify** — Never put all your money into a single company. Spread across multiple EIS or SEIS investments, or use a fund.
- **Check the company qualifies** — Ask for an advance assurance letter from HMRC confirming EIS or SEIS eligibility before investing.
- **Hold for the minimum period** — Selling before 3 years (EIS/SEIS) or 5 years (VCT) means you lose the income tax relief.
- **Get financial advice** — These are complex products. A financial adviser can help you assess suitability, tax position and portfolio fit.
- **Consider VCTs for easier access** — If you want tax-relieved exposure without the complexity of direct startup investing, VCTs offer a managed, listed alternative.
- **Use loss relief wisely** — If an EIS or SEIS investment fails, the loss relief means you only lose 45–50p for every £1 invested (depending on your tax rate).
