Identity theft is one of the fastest-growing crimes in the UK. Criminals steal your personal information to open bank accounts, take out loans, or make purchases in your name. In 2023, UK victims lost an estimated £4.4 billion to identity fraud. The good news is that most identity theft is preventable with simple, practical steps.

## What Is Identity Theft?

Identity theft happens when someone obtains your personal information — such as your name, date of birth, address, bank details, or National Insurance number — and uses it without your consent. They may use this information to:

- Open bank accounts or credit cards in your name
- Apply for loans or mortgages
- Make purchases or transfer money from your accounts
- Commit benefits fraud or tax fraud
- Obtain official documents like passports or driving licences

Identity theft is not just an inconvenience. It can damage your credit score, result in financial losses, and take months to resolve. In severe cases, victims face wrongful debt collection or criminal accusations for crimes they did not commit.

## Common Identity Theft Scams in the UK

Criminals use a variety of methods to steal your personal information. Here are the most common scams targeting UK residents.

### Phishing Emails

Phishing emails pretend to be from legitimate organisations — your bank, HMRC, or a retailer. They contain links to fake websites that look like the real thing. When you enter your login details or personal information, the criminals capture it.

**Warning signs:**
- Generic greetings like "Dear customer" instead of your name
- Urgent language pressuring you to act immediately
- Slight misspellings in email addresses (e.g., support@bank-secure.co.uk)
- Requests for passwords, PINs, or full security codes

### Fake Websites

Criminals create websites that mimic real retailers, banks, or government services. They often appear in paid search results above the genuine site. If you enter payment details, they steal your card information.

**How to spot them:**
- Check the URL carefully — look for subtle differences
- Look for the padlock symbol in the browser bar
- Be cautious of deals that seem too good to be true

### Phone Calls (Vishing)

Criminals call pretending to be from your bank, the police, or a technology company. They may claim your account has been compromised and ask you to "verify" your details or transfer money to a "safe account."

**Remember:** Your bank will never ask you to transfer money to a safe account, ask for your full PIN or password, or send someone to your home to collect your card.

### Postal Redirection

Criminals file a postal redirection order in your name, redirecting your post to their address. They then intercept bank statements, credit card offers, and other documents containing personal information.

### Social Engineering

Criminals gather information from social media profiles, public records, and casual conversations. They may pose as someone you know or a representative of a company you use.

**What they look for:**
- Your full name, date of birth, and address
- Mother's maiden name
- Pet names or children's names
- Workplace information
- Travel plans

### SIM Swapping

Criminals contact your mobile provider pretending to be you, requesting a SIM swap. Once they have your number on their SIM, they receive your two-factor authentication codes and can access your bank accounts.

## How to Protect Yourself

Protecting your identity does not require technical expertise. Simple habits make a significant difference.

### Use Strong, Unique Passwords

- Use a different password for every online account
- Make passwords at least 12 characters long with a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols
- Use a password manager to generate and store unique passwords
- Never reuse passwords across different accounts

### Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Two-factor authentication adds a second layer of security. Even if a criminal gets your password, they cannot access your account without the second factor.

- Enable 2FA on all banking, email, and social media accounts
- Use an authenticator app (like Google Authenticator or Authy) rather than SMS where possible
- Keep backup codes in a secure location

### Check Your Credit Report Regularly

Your credit report shows all accounts opened in your name and recent credit searches. Checking it regularly helps you spot fraud early.

- Check all three credit reference agencies: Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion
- Free services include ClearScore (Equifax), Credit Karma (TransUnion), and Money Saving Expert Credit Club (Experian)
- Look for accounts you do not recognise, searches you did not authorise, or address changes
- Check at least once a month

### Shred Sensitive Documents

- Shred bank statements, credit card offers, and bills before disposing of them
- Use a cross-cut shredder — strip-cut shredders can be reassembled
- Do not simply tear documents and put them in the recycling

### Do Not Click Links in Unsolicited Messages

- Never click links in unexpected emails or text messages
- Type the website address directly into your browser
- If in doubt, contact the organisation directly using the phone number on their official website or your bank card

### Verify Callers

- If someone calls claiming to be from your bank, hang up
- Wait five minutes to ensure the line is clear
- Call your bank back using the number on their website or your card
- Never give out your PIN, full password, or one-time codes to anyone who calls you

## Credit Freeze: Your Emergency Brake

If you suspect your identity has been stolen, you can request a credit freeze (also called a credit lock or fraud alert) from all three credit reference agencies. This prevents new credit accounts from being opened in your name.

### How to Freeze Your Credit

| Agency | How to Freeze | Contact |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| Experian | Online via your Experian account or by phone | 0344 481 0800 |
| Equifax | Online via your Equifax account or by phone | 0800 015 6291 |
| TransUnion | Online via your TransUnion account or by phone | 0330 024 7574 |

The freeze is free and can be lifted whenever you need to apply for credit. If you need to apply for a mortgage, credit card, or loan, temporarily lift the freeze, complete the application, and then freeze it again.

### When to Use a Credit Freeze

- You have received a data breach notification
- You suspect your personal information has been compromised
- You notice suspicious activity on your credit report
- You want to prevent fraud before it happens — you can freeze your credit as a precaution

## What to Do If Your Identity Is Stolen

If you discover that your identity has been stolen, act quickly to limit the damage.

### Step 1: Report to Action Fraud

Action Fraud is the UK's national fraud and cybercrime reporting centre.

- **Phone:** 0300 123 2040
- **Online:** actionfraud.police.uk
- Available Monday to Friday, 8am to 8pm

Reporting helps law enforcement track criminal activity and may help you recover losses.

### Step 2: Contact Your Banks and Card Providers

- Report the fraud immediately
- Ask them to freeze or close affected accounts
- Request new cards and account numbers
- Check for any unauthorised transactions and dispute them
- Under the Payment Services Regulations, you may be entitled to a refund if you acted promptly

### Step 3: Update All Passwords

- Change passwords for all accounts that may be affected
- Start with your email accounts, banking, and any accounts linked to the compromised information
- Enable two-factor authentication where it was not previously enabled

### Step 4: Check Your Credit Report

- Obtain reports from all three agencies: Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion
- Look for any accounts or searches you do not recognise
- Dispute any fraudulent entries with the credit reference agency

### Step 5: Contact the Police

File a police report to create an official record. This may be needed for insurance claims or to prove your identity was stolen.

### Step 6: Place a Fraud Alert

Ask all three credit reference agencies to add a fraud alert to your file. This alerts lenders to take extra care when verifying your identity before opening new accounts.

## Fraud Alerts on Bank Accounts

Most UK banks offer real-time fraud alerts that notify you of suspicious activity on your accounts. Set these up to catch fraud as soon as it happens.

### What Banks Typically Offer

- **Transaction alerts:** Text or push notifications for every transaction
- **Large transaction alerts:** Notifications for transactions above a set amount
- **International transaction alerts:** Notifications for transactions made abroad
- **Online shopping alerts:** Notifications for card-not-present transactions
- **Login alerts:** Notifications when your account is accessed online

### How to Set Up Alerts

Check your banking app or online banking settings. Most major UK banks — including Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds, NatWest, Santander, and Halifax — offer real-time notifications at no cost.

## Worked Example: John's Identity Theft

John is a 35-year-old accountant living in Manchester. One Monday morning, he receives an email that appears to be from his bank. The email says there has been suspicious activity on his account and asks him to click a link to verify his details.

The email looks legitimate — it has the bank's logo, correct colours, and his name. He clicks the link and is taken to a website that looks identical to his bank's login page. He enters his username and password. The site says "verification complete" and redirects him to the real bank website.

The next morning, John checks his bank app and sees that £5,000 has been transferred out of his account overnight. He also notices several small test transactions of £1 and £2 that he did not make.

### John's Response

**Immediate actions:**
1. He calls his bank using the number on the back of his debit card and reports the fraud. The bank freezes his account and begins an investigation.
2. He reports the fraud to Action Fraud online at actionfraud.police.uk.
3. He changes his banking password and enables two-factor authentication.

**Within 24 hours:**
4. He requests a credit freeze from all three credit reference agencies.
5. He checks his credit report on ClearScore, Credit Karma, and Experian for any unauthorised accounts.
6. He sets up real-time transaction alerts on all his bank accounts and credit cards.

**Within one week:**
7. His bank completes its investigation and refunds the £5,000 under the Payment Services Regulations.
8. He receives a new debit card and updated account details.
9. He reports the incident to the police for their records.

### What John Learned

- Never click links in emails — even if they look legitimate
- Always go directly to your bank's website by typing the address into your browser
- Set up two-factor authentication on all financial accounts
- Enable real-time transaction alerts
- Check your credit report regularly

## Tips for Staying Protected

Here are the key habits that keep your identity safe.

| Tip | Why It Matters |
|-----|----------------|
| Use strong, unique passwords | Prevents credential stuffing attacks |
| Enable two-factor authentication | Adds a second layer of security |
| Check your credit report monthly | Catches fraud early |
| Do not share personal information unnecessarily | Reduces your attack surface |
| Shred sensitive documents | Prevents mail theft fraud |
| Be sceptical of unsolicited contact | Reduces phishing risk |
| Verify callers independently | Prevents vishing attacks |
| Monitor bank transactions regularly | Catches unauthorised activity quickly |
| Use secure Wi-Fi networks | Prevents data interception |
| Keep software updated | Patches security vulnerabilities |

## Key Takeaways

- Identity theft cost UK victims an estimated £4.4 billion in 2023.
- Common scams include phishing emails, fake websites, phone calls, postal redirection, social engineering, and SIM swapping.
- Use strong, unique passwords and enable two-factor authentication on all accounts.
- Check your credit report monthly through free services like ClearScore, Credit Karma, or Money Saving Expert Credit Club.
- If your identity is stolen, report to Action Fraud, contact your banks, update passwords, and freeze your credit.
- Set up real-time fraud alerts on your bank accounts.
- Never click links in unsolicited emails or texts — go directly to the website instead.
