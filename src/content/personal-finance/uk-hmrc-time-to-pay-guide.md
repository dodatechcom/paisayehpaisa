Falling behind on tax is stressful, but HMRC offers a structured way to catch up. A Time to Pay (TTP) arrangement lets you spread your tax debt over monthly instalments rather than paying everything at once. Here is how it works, who qualifies, and what to expect when you call.

## What Is HMRC Time to Pay?

Time to Pay is an arrangement between you and HMRC that allows you to pay an outstanding tax debt in monthly instalments instead of a lump sum. It is available for:

- Income tax (including Self Assessment)
- VAT
- PAYE (PAYE arrears for employers)
- Corporation tax

TTP is not a write-off. You will still pay the full amount owed plus interest. It simply gives you breathing room to manage the debt without enforcement action.

## Who Can Use Time to Pay?

Both individuals and businesses can apply for a TTP arrangement. The key requirements are:

- You must be able to pay the debt in full within 12 months. In some cases HMRC may extend this to 24 months.
- You must have a reasonable means to pay — HMRC will assess your income, expenses, and assets.
- You must contact HMRC before any enforcement action has started (though it may still be possible after).
- You cannot set up TTP for debts that are already being collected through a formal insolvency process.

If your debt is under £30,000 and you have no other payment plans in place, you may be able to set up TTP entirely online through GOV.UK.

## How to Arrange a TTP Plan

### Online for debts under £30,000

HMRC provides a self-service online payment plan for smaller debts. You can access it through your personal tax account on GOV.UK. The system will:

1. Ask you to confirm the debt you want to pay.
2. Show you a suggested repayment schedule.
3. Let you choose a monthly payment you can afford.
4. Provide instant approval if you meet the criteria.

This is the quickest option. No phone calls, no waiting. You can set up a direct debit and the plan starts immediately.

### Phone for larger debts or complex situations

For debts over £30,000, or if your circumstances are more complicated, you need to call HMRC's Payment Support Service.

**HMRC Payment Support Service:** 0300 200 3835

When you call, be prepared to:

- Explain why you cannot pay in full.
- Provide details of your income and essential outgoings.
- Suggest a monthly payment amount you can realistically afford.

HMRC will review your situation and either accept your proposal, suggest an alternative amount, or ask for supporting documents (such as bank statements or a breakdown of expenses).

### Mental health helpline

If you are struggling with the emotional impact of tax debt, HMRC has a dedicated mental health helpline that can provide extra support.

**HMRC Mental Health Helpline:** 0300 200 3300

This is a separate team trained to handle calls with sensitivity. They can also help set up payment arrangements.

## What HMRC Will Accept

HMRC's approach is affordability-based. They are not trying to push you into financial difficulty — they want to recover the debt over a realistic timeframe.

When you contact HMRC, they will ask about:

- Your monthly income (employment, self-employment, or benefits)
- Essential expenses (rent or mortgage, council tax, utilities, food, transport)
- Any assets you hold (savings, property, vehicles)
- Other debts you are repaying

Based on this, they will agree to a monthly payment you can sustain. If your circumstances change later — for example, you lose your job or your income drops — you can contact HMRC again to review the arrangement.

## Interest on Late Payments

HMRC charges interest on overdue tax. The current rates (as of 2026) are:

| Tax type | Interest rate |
|---|---|
| Income tax | 7.75% |
| VAT | 2.5% |
| Corporation tax | 7.75% |

Interest accrues daily from the original due date. If you set up a TTP arrangement, interest continues to accrue on the outstanding balance until the debt is fully cleared. The interest is added to your total debt, so your monthly payments cover both the original tax and the accumulated interest.

## Penalties and Late Filing

Setting up a TTP arrangement does not cancel late filing penalties. If you submitted your tax return or VAT return late, those penalties still apply. However, TTP can reduce or cancel late payment penalties. HMRC is more likely to waive late payment penalties if you proactively contact them and set up a reasonable arrangement.

The key point: contacting HMRC early and arranging a plan shows goodwill, which works in your favour if you need to appeal a penalty later.

## What Happens If You Cannot Pay

If you do not contact HMRC and do not pay, enforcement action can follow:

- **Court orders** — HMRC can apply for a liability order through the magistrates' court.
- **Bailiffs** — County Court Judgment (CCJ) enforcement agents can visit to seize goods.
- **Charging orders** — HMRC can place a charge on your property.
- **Bankruptcy proceedings** — for debts over £5,000.
- **Deduction from earnings** — your employer may be ordered to deduct payments from your salary.

HMRC prefers to avoid these steps. They would rather you contact them and set up a plan. Enforcement is a last resort.

## Worked Example: Self-Employed Plumber

Meet James. He is a self-employed plumber who owes £8,000 in income tax from his last Self Assessment.

**James's financial situation:**

- Monthly income: £3,000
- Essential expenses: £2,200 (rent, food, transport, utilities, council tax)
- Disposable income: £800
- Other debt repayments: £600

James calls HMRC's Payment Support Service and explains he can afford £200 per month. HMRC reviews his bank statements and accepts the proposal.

**The arrangement:**

- Debt: £8,000
- Monthly payment: £200
- Duration: 40 months
- Interest rate: 7.75% per year
- Estimated total interest: approximately £1,300 over the 40 months

After 40 months, James has cleared the full £8,000 plus the accrued interest. During the arrangement, he continues to file his tax returns on time and keeps up with his current tax obligations. HMRC does not take any enforcement action.

## Tips for Managing HMRC Debt

1. **Contact HMRC early.** The sooner you get in touch, the more options you have. Waiting until enforcement has started limits your choices.
2. **Be honest about your finances.** HMRC will ask for details and may request evidence. Providing accurate information speeds up the process.
3. **Keep records.** Save copies of any correspondence, bank statements, and details of your TTP agreement.
4. **Set up a direct debit.** This ensures your monthly payments go out on time and avoids missing a payment, which could void your arrangement.
5. **Review if circumstances change.** If your income drops or expenses rise, contact HMRC to renegotiate the plan.
6. **Get advice if your situation is complex.** Citizens Advice, Tax Aid, and professional accountants can help you understand your options and negotiate with HMRC on your behalf.
7. **Stay current on new obligations.** Your TTP arrangement covers the old debt. You still need to pay current tax on time — otherwise you will end up with a new debt on top of the old one.
