If you are employed in the UK and cannot work because of illness, you are likely entitled to Statutory Sick Pay (SSP). This is the minimum amount your employer must pay you when you are sick. Many employers offer more than the statutory minimum, but SSP sets the legal baseline. This guide explains what SSP is, who qualifies, how much you get, and what to do if you are unable to work.

## What Is Statutory Sick Pay?

Statutory Sick Pay is a legal minimum payment that employers must provide to eligible employees who are too ill to work. It is not a government benefit paid directly to you — your employer pays it, and they may be able to reclaim some or all of it from HMRC.

### SSP rates (2024/25)

- **Weekly rate:** £116.75
- **Maximum duration:** Up to 28 weeks
- **Payment frequency:** Paid in the same way as your normal wages (weekly or monthly)

The SSP rate is reviewed annually by the government. For the 2024/25 tax year (April 2024 to April 2025), the weekly rate is £116.75.

## Am I Eligible for SSP?

Not everyone qualifies for SSP. You must meet all of the following conditions:

### Eligibility criteria

- **Employment status:** You must be classified as an employee (not self-employed, not a agency worker without a contract, not in military service).
- **Earnings threshold:** You must earn at least £123 per week on average. This is calculated over an 8-week reference period.
- **Waiting period:** You must be off work ill for at least 3 consecutive days (called a "waiting period"). These 3 days include non-working days — so if you fall ill on a Friday and do not work weekends, the weekend counts towards the waiting period.
- **Notification:** You must tell your employer you are unable to work due to illness. Different employers have different deadlines, but you generally have up to 7 days to notify them.

If you do not meet the earnings threshold, your employer does not have to pay you SSP, but you may be eligible for other benefits such as New Style Employment and Support Allowance (ESA).

## How to Claim SSP

Claiming SSP is straightforward, but there are deadlines and documentation requirements you need to follow.

### Step 1: Notify your employer

Tell your employer as soon as possible that you are unable to work due to illness. Check your employment contract or employee handbook for the specific notification procedure — some employers require you to call, email, or use an online absence reporting system.

### Step 2: Provide medical evidence

- For the first 7 days of absence, you do not usually need a doctor's note (fit note). You can self-certify by completing a self-certification form (SC2), which your employer may provide.
- After 7 days of continuous absence, your employer can request a fit note from your GP. This is sometimes called a sick note or doctor's note.
- If your GP issues a fit note, it will state whether you are "not fit for work" or "may be fit for work" with suggested adjustments.

### Step 3: SSP is calculated and paid

Your employer calculates your SSP based on your average weekly earnings over the relevant reference period. SSP is paid on the normal payday, alongside any other wages you are owed.

### Deadlines

- You must notify your employer within the deadline set by your contract (usually 7 days).
- Your employer must pay SSP on or before your normal payday.
- If your employer refuses to pay SSP, you can contact HMRC's statutory payment dispute resolution service.

## Employer Top-Up: Contractual Sick Pay

The £116.75 weekly SSP rate is the legal minimum. Many employers offer more generous sick pay as part of your employment contract. This is known as contractual sick pay or enhanced sick pay.

### Common employer schemes

- **Full pay for a set period:** Some employers pay your full salary for the first few weeks or months of absence, then switch to SSP.
- **Half pay schemes:** Some employers pay 50% of your salary for a defined period.
- **Occupational sick pay:** Larger employers, particularly in the public sector, often have occupational sick pay schemes that exceed SSP.

### What to do

- **Check your contract:** Your employment contract, staff handbook, or intranet should detail your employer's sick pay policy.
- **Ask HR:** If you are unsure, contact your HR department before you need to take sick leave.
- **Know the difference:** Contractual sick pay is a contractual right. SSP is a statutory right. If your employer's contractual scheme ends, you may still be entitled to SSP for the remainder of the 28-week period.

## SSP and Other Benefits

If you receive SSP, it counts as taxable income. This can affect other benefits you may be claiming.

### Universal Credit

SSP is treated as earnings for Universal Credit purposes. If you are receiving Universal Credit, your SSP will be taken into account in your monthly assessment. This may reduce your Universal Credit payment, but it will not usually eliminate it entirely.

### Housing Benefit

If you receive Housing Benefit, SSP is counted as income. Your Housing Benefit payment may be reduced depending on how much SSP you receive and your other income.

### Council Tax Reduction

Some local authorities treat SSP as income when calculating council tax reduction. Check with your local council.

### Tax and National Insurance

SSP is subject to tax and National Insurance contributions, just like your normal wages. Your employer deducts these automatically.

## Self-Employed: No SSP Available

Statutory Sick Pay is only available to employees. If you are self-employed, you do not qualify for SSP. However, there are alternative options:

### New Style Employment and Support Allowance (ESA)

If you are self-employed and too ill to work, you may be able to claim New Style ESA. This is a contributory benefit based on your National Insurance record. You can claim it regardless of your savings or your partner's income.

### Other options

- **Universal Credit:** If your income drops due to illness, you may be eligible for Universal Credit.
- **Private sick pay insurance:** Some self-employed people take out income protection or sickness insurance policies.
- **Savings:** This is where an emergency fund becomes essential — ideally 3 to 6 months of living expenses.

## Worked Example: Tom the Warehouse Worker

Tom works in a warehouse and earns £300 per week. He breaks his leg and is off work for 8 weeks. His employer pays full salary for the first 4 weeks, then switches to SSP for the remaining 4 weeks.

### Calculation

| Period | Payment | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Weeks 1–4 (employer full pay) | £300 × 4 | £1,200 |
| Weeks 5–8 (SSP at £116.75/week) | £116.75 × 4 | £467 |
| **Total over 8 weeks** | | **£1,667** |

### Key points

- Tom earns above the £123/week threshold, so he qualifies for SSP.
- His employer's contractual sick pay covers the first 4 weeks at full salary.
- After 4 weeks, SSP kicks in for the remaining period.
- Tom's total income over 8 weeks is £1,667, which is less than he would have earned working (£2,400), but it provides essential financial support during his recovery.
- Tom should check whether his employer offers any additional support, such as access to an Employee Assistance Programme or occupational health referral.

## Tips for Making the Most of SSP

- **Check your contract first.** Your employer may offer more generous sick pay than SSP. Always check your contract and staff handbook.
- **Keep your GP notes.** If you are off work for more than 7 days, you will need a fit note. Keep copies of all medical documentation.
- **Apply even if you are unsure.** If you think you might be eligible, apply anyway. Your employer can confirm your entitlement.
- **Claim benefits if SSP is not enough.** If your income drops significantly, check whether you are eligible for Universal Credit, Housing Benefit, or other support.
- **Know your rights.** Your employer cannot dismiss you for taking sick leave if you are eligible for SSP. If you believe you have been treated unfairly, contact Acas (Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service) for free advice.
- **Plan ahead.** If possible, build an emergency fund to cover the gap between your normal salary and SSP.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can my employer refuse to pay SSP?

Your employer can only refuse SSP if you do not meet the eligibility criteria — for example, if you earn below £123 per week, or if you have not been off work for at least 3 consecutive days. If you believe you are eligible and your employer refuses to pay, contact HMRC.

### Does SSP apply to part-time workers?

Yes. Part-time workers are eligible for SSP as long as they earn at least £123 per week on average and meet the other criteria.

### Can I get SSP if I have been dismissed?

If you were dismissed and are now ill, you may not be entitled to SSP from your former employer. However, you may be eligible for New Style ESA or Universal Credit.

### How long does SSP last?

SSP is paid for up to 28 weeks. After 28 weeks, you may be eligible for other benefits such as New Style ESA.

### Is SSP taxable?

Yes. SSP is subject to income tax and National Insurance, just like your normal wages.
