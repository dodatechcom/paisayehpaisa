If you were born in the UK between 1 September 2002 and 2 January 2011, the government gave you free money. You might not know about it, but it is sitting there waiting for you. Billions of pounds in Child Trust Funds remain unclaimed. This guide explains what Child Trust Funds are, how to find yours, and how to get your hands on the money.

## What Were Child Trust Funds?

Child Trust Funds (CTFs) were a UK government savings scheme that ran from **2005 to 2011**. Every child born between **1 September 2002 and 2 January 2011** received a voucher from the government to open a Child Trust Fund account.

### How It Worked

- The government gave every eligible child a **voucher worth £250** (or £500 for children from low-income families)
- Parents or guardians used the voucher to open a CTF account with an approved provider
- The money was invested in a tax-free savings account
- Parents, family, and friends could contribute up to **£1,200 per year**
- The money was locked away until the child turned **18**

### Why Did It Stop?

The CTF scheme was closed in 2011. New contributions were no longer accepted from January 2011. The government replaced it with the **Junior ISA (JISA)**, which has similar tax benefits but is not funded by government vouchers.

## If You Never Opened an Account

Many parents did not open a CTF account. If the voucher was not used, **HMRC held the money**. The government invested it on your behalf.

### What Happened to the Money

- HMRC invested unclaimed vouchers in a default CTF
- The money has been growing since 2005-2011
- It is worth more than the original £250 or £500
- You can claim it now if you are 18 or over

### How Much Is It Worth?

The default CTF accounts have been invested in a low-risk fund. The exact value depends on when you were born and market performance, but many unclaimed CTFs are now worth **£500 to £1,500 or more**.

## Finding Your Child Trust Fund

You can find your CTF using the government's free online tool.

### How to Check

1. Go to **[gov.uk/child-trust-fund](https://www.gov.uk/child-trust-fund)**
2. Enter your details:
   - Full name
   - Date of birth
   - National Insurance number
3. HMRC will tell you:
   - Which provider holds your CTF
   - Your account number
   - How to contact the provider

### What If You Do Not Have a National Insurance Number?

You can still check. HMRC may ask for additional identification, such as:

- Passport number
- P60 or payslip
- Proof of address

### What If You Were Adopted or Had a Name Change?

Contact HMRC directly if your details have changed. They can trace your CTF using other information.

## Claiming Your Money

Once you find your CTF provider, you need to contact them to access the money.

### When Can You Claim?

- Your CTF matures when you turn **18**
- If you turned 18 in **2023, 2024, 2025, or 2026**, your CTF has matured or is about to mature
- You can withdraw the money once you have proof of identity

### What to Do

1. **Contact the provider** — call or go online to verify your identity
2. **Choose what to do with the money:**
   - **Withdraw it** — take the full amount as cash
   - **Transfer to an ISA** — move it to a stocks and shares ISA or cash ISA to keep the tax-free benefits
   - **Leave it invested** — some providers allow you to keep the money invested in a GIA (General Investment Account)
3. **Provide ID** — you will need a passport, driving licence, or other ID

### What If the Provider Has Closed?

If your CTF provider no longer exists, your account has been transferred to another provider. HMRC can tell you which provider now holds your account.

## How Much Is a Child Trust Fund Worth?

CTF values vary depending on:

- The original government contribution (£250 or £500)
- Any additional contributions from parents or family
- Investment performance over the years
- Charges and fees

### Average Values

| Type | Typical Value |
|---|---|
| Default CTF (no contributions) | £500 - £1,500 |
| Average CTF with contributions | £2,000 - £3,000 |
| Well-funded CTF | £5,000+ |

### Unclaimed Money

It is estimated that **£1.7 billion** in Child Trust Funds remain unclaimed. That is money belonging to young adults who may not even know it exists. If you are in the right age bracket, it is worth checking.

## CTF vs Junior ISA (JISA)

The Child Trust Fund was replaced by the Junior ISA in 2011. Here is how they compare.

| Feature | CTF | JISA |
|---|---|---|
| Years available | 2005 - 2011 | 2011 - present |
| Government voucher | Yes (£250-£500) | No |
| Annual contribution limit | £1,200 | £9,000 (2025-26) |
| Tax-free growth | Yes | Yes |
| Access age | 18 | 18 |
| Can still contribute | No (closed to new contributions) | Yes |

### Key Difference

The main difference is that CTFs received a government contribution, while JISAs do not. Both are tax-free savings accounts for children.

### Can You Transfer a CTF to a JISA?

You cannot transfer a CTF to a JISA. However, you can transfer a matured CTF to an adult ISA once you turn 18.

## Tax Treatment

Child Trust Funds are **completely tax-free**.

- No Income Tax on interest or dividends
- No Capital Gains Tax on growth
- No tax on withdrawals
- The money belongs to the child, not the parents

Once you withdraw the money at 18, it is yours to use however you like. There is no tax to pay.

## Worked Example: Sophie's Discovery

Sophie is 22 years old. Her parents mentioned that the government gave her money when she was born, but they never opened an account. Sophie decides to check.

### Step 1: Check Online

Sophie goes to **gov.uk/child-trust-fund** and enters her details:

- Name: Sophie Williams
- Date of birth: 15 March 2004
- National Insurance number: QQ 12 34 56 C

### Step 2: Find the Provider

HMRC tells Sophie her CTF is with **OneFamily**. She is given her account number and contact details.

### Step 3: Contact the Provider

Sophie calls OneFamily and verifies her identity with her passport. They tell her:

- Account value: **£2,500**
- This includes the original £500 government voucher and growth over 18 years
- Sophie can withdraw the money or transfer it to an ISA

### Step 4: Get the Money

Sophie chooses to withdraw the full £2,500. The money is transferred to her bank account within 5 working days.

### Step 5: Use the Money

Sophie uses the £2,500 as part of her deposit on her first flat. The money she did not know existed helped her get on the property ladder.

## What to Do If You Think You Had a CTF

Follow this checklist:

- [ ] Check if you were born between 1 September 2002 and 2 January 2011
- [ ] Ask your parents if they received a CTF voucher
- [ ] Go to **gov.uk/child-trust-fund** and enter your details
- [ ] If you find a provider, contact them to check the value
- [ ] Decide whether to withdraw or transfer to an ISA
- [ ] If you are under 18, the money is still locked away — check again when you turn 18

## Tips

1. **Check if you had a CTF** — if you were born 2002-2011, it is worth looking
2. **Ask your parents** — they may remember receiving a voucher or opening an account
3. **Use the gov.uk tool** — it is free and takes 5 minutes
4. **Check before you turn 18** — some providers contact you before maturity, but do not rely on this
5. **Consider transferring to an ISA** — keep the tax-free benefits going
6. **Get advice on the best use** — speak to a financial adviser if you are unsure what to do with the money
7. **Do not pay to find your CTF** — the gov.uk tool is free. Some companies charge fees to trace CTFs — you do not need them
