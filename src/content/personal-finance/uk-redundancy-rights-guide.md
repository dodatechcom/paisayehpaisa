Being made redundant is one of the most stressful events in anyone's working life. Understanding your rights can help you navigate the process with confidence and make sure you receive everything you are entitled to. This guide covers statutory redundancy pay, notice periods, tax treatment, and the financial steps you should take.

## Statutory Redundancy Pay

If you have been employed by your current employer for **two years or more**, you are legally entitled to statutory redundancy pay. The amount is based on three factors: your age, your length of service, and your weekly pay (subject to a cap).

### How It Is Calculated

| Age when redundancy takes effect | Amount per year of service |
|---|---|
| Under 22 | 0.5 week's pay |
| 22–40 | 1 week's pay |
| 41 and over | 1.5 weeks' pay |

Your weekly pay is capped at **£643** (2024/25). The maximum statutory redundancy payment is **£19,290**.

Your length of service is counted from your start date up to and including the date your employment ends. Service before age 16 and after 41 does not count.

### Example Calculation

For a 38-year-old with 8 years of service earning £643 per week:

- Years aged 22–40: 8 years × 1 week's pay = 8 weeks' pay
- Total: 8 × £643 = **£5,144**

### Can You Get More?

Some employers offer enhanced redundancy pay as part of your contract. Check your employment contract, staff handbook, or ask HR. If your contract promises more than the statutory amount, you are legally entitled to that higher amount.

## Notice Period

You are entitled to a statutory minimum notice period based on how long you have worked for your employer:

| Length of service | Minimum notice period |
|---|---|
| 1 month to 2 years | 1 week |
| 2 to 12 years | 1 week per year of service |
| 12 years or more | 12 weeks |

Your contract may give you longer notice than the statutory minimum — check your terms. Your employer may choose to pay you in lieu of notice (PILON) instead of working it, but the notice pay is still due.

## Consultation

If your employer is making **20 or more redundancies** at one establishment within 90 days, they must legally consult with employees or recognised trade union representatives:

| Number of redundancies | Minimum consultation period |
|---|---|
| 20–99 redundancies | 30 days |
| 100 or more redundancies | 45 days |

During consultation, your employer must share the reasons for redundancy, how many people are affected, and how they will select who is made redundant. They must also consider alternatives, such as redeployment or voluntary redundancy.

## Tax on Redundancy Pay

The tax treatment of redundancy payments depends on what the payment is for:

- **Statutory or contractual redundancy pay** — the first **£30,000 is tax-free**. Any amount above £30,000 is taxed as employment income
- **Notice pay** — taxed as normal employment income, unless it is paid as a lump sum from an approved scheme
- **Holiday pay** — taxed as normal employment income
- **Payment in lieu of notice (PILON)** — usually taxed as normal employment income

If your total redundancy package is under £30,000, you may not owe any tax at all. If it exceeds £30,000, you will pay tax on the excess at your marginal rate.

## Settlement Agreements

Your employer may offer you a **settlement agreement** (formerly called a compromise agreement). This is a legally binding contract where you agree to waive your right to bring an employment claim in exchange for a financial package.

Settlement agreements often include:

- Enhanced redundancy pay above the statutory minimum
- An ex gratia payment
- Agreed references
- Payment of legal fees for independent legal advice

You are **not obliged** to sign a settlement agreement. You have **21 days** to consider the offer (or 45 days if more than 20 employees are being made redundant at the same time). Always get independent legal advice before signing — your employer should pay for this.

## Unfair Dismissal and Redundancy Selection

Redundancy must be a **genuine** reason for dismissal. Your employer cannot select you for redundancy based on:

- Age, gender, race, or religion
- Disability
- Pregnancy or maternity leave
- Trade union membership or activity
- Whistleblowing
- Part-time or fixed-term status

If you believe you have been unfairly selected, you may have a claim for unfair dismissal at an employment tribunal. You generally need at least **two years' continuous service** to bring a claim (with some exceptions).

## Pension

Your workplace pension continues during your notice period. Your employer must continue making their contributions if your contract requires it.

When you leave, you have several options for your pension pot:

- **Leave it where it is** — your pension remains invested and you can access it from age 55 (57 from 2028)
- **Transfer it** — move it to a new employer's pension or a personal pension
- **Take a cash sum** — you can usually take up to 25% tax-free as a pension commencement lump sum

Check your pension scheme's terms, as some may charge fees for leaving or have special rules for redundancy.

## Benefits

If you are made redundant, you can claim:

- **Universal Credit** — available if you are on a low income or have no income. There are usually **five waiting days** before payment begins (seven for new claims)
- **New Style Jobseeker's Allowance (JSA)** — £90.50 per week (2024/25) if you have paid enough National Insurance contributions. There is usually a **three-day waiting period**
- **Housing Benefit or the housing element of Universal Credit** — to help with rent if you are on a low income

You can claim Universal Credit and New Style JSA at the same time. The JSA amount is deducted from your Universal Credit payment.

## Worked Example: David

David is 38 years old, has worked for his employer for 8 years, and earns £40,000 per year.

**Step 1: Statutory redundancy pay**

- Weekly pay: £40,000 ÷ 52 = £769.23, but capped at £643
- David is aged 22–40 for all 8 years: 8 × 1 week's pay = 8 weeks
- Statutory redundancy: 8 × £643 = **£5,144** (tax-free, under £30,000)

**Step 2: Notice pay**

- David's contract entitles him to 8 weeks' notice
- Notice pay: 8 × £769.23 = **£6,154**
- Taxed as normal employment income

**Step 3: Holiday pay**

- David has 5 days accrued annual leave
- Holiday pay: 5 × £153.85 = **£769**
- Taxed as normal employment income

**David's total package:**

| Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| Statutory redundancy pay | £5,144 |
| Notice pay | £6,154 |
| Holiday pay | £769 |
| **Total** | **£12,067** |

Of this total, the first £30,000 is tax-free, so David's redundancy pay is entirely tax-free. His notice pay and holiday pay are taxed as normal income through PAYE.

## Tips for Managing Redundancy

- **Check your contract** for enhanced redundancy pay or other entitlements — many employers offer more than the statutory minimum
- **Negotiate** if you are offered a settlement agreement — there may be room for improvement, especially if you have a strong case
- **Get legal advice** before signing any settlement agreement — your employer should pay for this, and it is always worth an independent review
- **Claim benefits immediately** — do not wait. Universal Credit and JSA can be claimed online at gov.uk, and JSA can help cover gaps while your Universal Credit claim is processed
- **Update your CV and LinkedIn profile** straight away — the sooner you start looking, the sooner you find the right role
- **Network** — let contacts know you are looking for work. Many jobs are filled through referrals before they are advertised
- **Check your pension** and get advice on your options before making any decisions
