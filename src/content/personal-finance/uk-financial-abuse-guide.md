Financial abuse is one of the most common yet least recognised forms of domestic abuse in the UK. It happens when someone controls your ability to acquire, use, or maintain money. If your partner or ex-partner controls your finances, prevents you from working, or runs up debt in your name, you are not alone — and there is help available.

## What Is Financial Abuse?

Financial abuse is a pattern of controlling behaviour that restricts a victim's access to money or financial independence. It is recognised as a form of coercive control under the Serious Crime Act 2015, which made it a criminal offence in England and Wales.

Financial abuse can happen in any relationship — married couples, civil partners, unmarried partners, parent and child, or between any family members. It affects people of all ages, incomes, and backgrounds.

### Key facts about financial abuse in the UK

- An estimated 2.3 million adults in England and Wales experienced domestic abuse in the year ending March 2022, with financial abuse reported in many of these cases.
- 60% of domestic abuse victims report that their partner used financial abuse as a tactic of control.
- Financial abuse is often one of the earliest signs of domestic abuse, occurring before physical violence begins.
- Men can be victims too — the Men's Advice Line reports that financial control is a common form of abuse against male victims.

Financial abuse does not require physical violence. Controlling the household budget, monitoring every purchase, or preventing someone from working can all constitute financial abuse.

## Signs of Financial Abuse

Financial abuse can take many forms. You may recognise some of these behaviours in your own relationship.

### Common signs include

- **Controlling all money:** Your partner takes charge of all household income, gives you an "allowance," or refuses to give you access to bank accounts.
- **Checking spending:** Your partner scrutinises every purchase you make, demands receipts, or interrogates you about small amounts.
- **Preventing work:** Your partner stops you from getting a job, sabotages your career, or forces you to quit.
- **Running up debt in your name:** Your partner takes out credit cards, loans, or mortgages in your name without your knowledge or consent.
- **Taking your wages:** Your partner demands your salary, redirects your income, or takes money you have earned.
- **Blocking bank account access:** Your partner changes passwords, removes you from joint accounts, or moves money without your permission.
- **Gaslighting about money:** Your partner denies that financial problems exist, claims you are "bad with money," or makes you doubt your own financial reality.
- **Forced dependency:** Your partner creates a situation where you are entirely financially dependent on them.
- **Refusing to contribute:** Your partner refuses to pay for household expenses while spending freely on themselves.
- **Hiding assets:** Your partner conceals income, investments, or property to prevent you from accessing shared resources.

## Legal Protection in the UK

Financial abuse and coercive control are taken seriously under UK law.

### Coercive control

Since 2015, the Serious Crime Act has made coercive or controlling behaviour a criminal offence. This includes patterns of behaviour that:

- Are used to punish, frighten, or humiliate
- Are used to isolate the victim from friends, family, or support
- Result in the victim being deprived of basic needs, including money

A conviction for coercive control carries a maximum sentence of **5 years imprisonment** and/or a fine.

### Court orders

If you are leaving an abusive partner, the family courts can make several orders to protect you:

- **Non-molestation order:** Prohibits your partner from using or threatening violence, harassing you, or coming within a certain distance of you.
- **Occupation order:** Regulates who can live in the family home, potentially excluding your abusive partner.
- **Prohibited steps order:** Prevents your partner from taking children out of the country or making major decisions about them.

### Divorce and financial settlements

Financial abuse can be grounds for divorce. When the court decides on a financial settlement, it can take into account:

- Hidden debts run up by your partner
- The impact of financial abuse on your earning capacity
- Compensation for unfair financial treatment during the marriage

The court has wide discretion to make orders that ensure a fair outcome, and financial abuse is a relevant factor in how assets are divided.

## Practical Steps to Separate Safely

Leaving an abusive relationship requires careful planning, especially when money is involved.

### 1. Open a new bank account

Open a new bank account in your name only at a different bank from any joint accounts. Choose a bank your partner does not bank with. You can open an account online or in branch — you will need photo ID and proof of address.

**Tips for opening your account:**
- Use a friend's address or your workplace address if you are concerned about post arriving at home.
- Ask the bank to send all correspondence by email only.
- Choose online-only banking so there are no paper statements.
- Set up the account on a device your partner does not have access to.

### 2. Change your passwords

Change passwords for all your online accounts — banking, email, social media, shopping. Use two-factor authentication where possible. If you share devices, try to use private browsing or a different device.

### 3. Get your credit report

Contact all three credit reference agencies to check for debts you do not recognise:
- **Experian:** experian.co.uk
- **Equifax:** equifax.co.uk
- **TransUnion:** transunion.co.uk

Under GDPR, you have the right to see all information held about you. If you find debts in your name that you did not authorise, contact the lender and report the fraud to Action Fraud (0300 123 2040).

### 4. Contact a helpline

The **National Domestic Abuse Helpline** on **0808 2000 247** is free, confidential, and available 24 hours a day. They can help you safety plan, find local support, and refer you to a refuge if needed.

### 5. Get legal advice

Contact a solicitor who specialises in family law. Many solicitors offer a free initial consultation. You may be eligible for legal aid if you are on a low income or have been the victim of domestic abuse.

## Emergency Money and Support

If you are leaving an abusive relationship, you may need emergency financial support.

### Benefits

- **Universal Credit:** If you have no income or savings below £16,000, you can claim Universal Credit. Contact the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) on 0800 328 5644.
- **Housing Benefit or the housing element of Universal Credit:** Can help with rent payments.
- **Council Tax Reduction:** You may get a discount if you are on a low income or living alone.
- **Child Benefit:** If you have children, you can claim Child Benefit.

### Refuge space

Refuge spaces are available across the UK for people fleeing domestic abuse. They provide safe accommodation and support. Contact the National Domestic Abuse Helpline or visit refuge.org.uk to find your nearest refuge.

### Emergency Relief Fund

Your local council may have an Emergency Relief Fund that can provide short-term financial help for essentials like food, transport, or clothing. Contact your local council directly.

### Sanctuary Scheme

The Sanctuary Scheme provides home security measures for victims of domestic abuse. This can include reinforced doors, locks, and alarm systems. Contact your local council or police for a referral.

## Money Mule Scams

Predators target vulnerable people, including those fleeing abusive relationships, to launder money through their bank accounts. This is known as a money mule scam.

### How it works

Someone approaches you — often online, through social media, or through someone you think you can trust — and asks you to receive money into your bank account and transfer it elsewhere. They may offer you a commission or claim it is for a legitimate purpose.

### The consequences

- Your bank account will be closed.
- You will find it extremely difficult to open a new account.
- You could get a criminal record for money laundering.
- You could face a prison sentence of up to 14 years.

### What to do

Never allow anyone to use your bank account, even if you know the person. If you are struggling financially, there are legitimate sources of help listed in this article. Report any suspicious approaches to Action Fraud on 0300 123 2040.

## Worked Example: Sarah's Story

Sarah is 34 and has been married for 8 years. Her partner controls all the finances. He earns £45,000 a year and transfers Sarah £100 a week for "household expenses." She has no access to their joint bank account and no idea how much money they have.

### Step 1: Opening a secret bank account

Sarah opens a new bank account online using her own name and her workplace address. She sets up online banking on her phone and asks the bank to send all correspondence by email.

### Step 2: Checking her credit report

Sarah checks her credit report and discovers a credit card and a personal loan totalling £5,000 in her name — neither of which she authorised. Her partner has been using these accounts without her knowledge.

### Step 3: Getting help

Sarah calls the National Domestic Abuse Helpline (0808 2000 247). They help her create a safety plan and refer her to a local refuge. She also contacts Citizens Advice for free legal advice.

### Step 4: Leaving safely

Sarah moves into a refuge with her two children. She applies for Universal Credit and receives emergency support from her local council.

### Step 5: After separation

Sarah's solicitor helps her apply for a non-molestation order. In the divorce proceedings, the court takes into account the hidden debt. The divorce settlement awards Sarah a larger share of the assets to compensate for the £5,000 debt run up in her name.

Sarah's story shows that leaving is possible, even when finances are completely controlled by a partner.

## Tips for Protecting Yourself

- **You are not alone.** Millions of people in the UK have experienced financial abuse. Support is available.
- **Keep records of abuse.** Save messages, emails, and screenshots that show controlling behaviour. Write a diary of incidents. This can be used as evidence in court.
- **Seek legal advice.** A family law solicitor can advise you on your rights and help you get court orders for protection.
- **Contact a helpline.** The National Domestic Abuse Helpline (0808 2000 247) can help you safety plan and find local support.
- **Open your own bank account.** Having money that is entirely in your name is a crucial step towards independence.
- **Check your credit report.** Find out if debts have been taken out in your name and report any fraud to Action Fraud.
- **Tell someone you trust.** A friend, family member, teacher, or colleague can provide emotional support and practical help.
- **Plan your exit carefully.** Do not rush to leave if it is not safe. Work with a helpline to create a safety plan before you go.
- **Know your rights.** Coercive control is a criminal offence. You do not have to tolerate it.
