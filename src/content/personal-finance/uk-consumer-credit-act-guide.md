The Consumer Credit Act 1974 is one of the most important pieces of consumer protection legislation in the UK. It sets out your rights when you borrow money — and, crucially, what happens when a lender fails to follow the rules. Understanding these rights can save you thousands of pounds and give you breathing space when debt becomes unmanageable.

## What the Consumer Credit Act Covers

The Act applies to **regulated credit agreements**, which include:

- Credit cards
- Personal loans
- Overdrafts
- Store cards
- Hire purchase agreements
- Conditional sale agreements
- Logbook loans

### What It Does NOT Cover

- Mortgages (regulated by the FCA under separate rules)
- Insurance products
- Lending by certain unregulated entities

If your borrowing falls under a regulated agreement, the creditor must follow strict rules — or face consequences.

## Section 127: When Agreements Are Unenforceable

**Section 127** of the Consumer Credit Act is one of the most powerful consumer protections available. It states that if a creditor fails to include certain **prescribed terms** in the agreement, the agreement may be **unenforceable**.

This does not mean the debt disappears — but it means the creditor cannot take enforcement action through the courts without first getting permission.

### Prescribed Terms That Must Be Included

Every regulated credit agreement must clearly state:

- **Amount of credit** — the total sum borrowed
- **APR** — the annual percentage rate, including all charges
- **Repayment schedule** — when and how repayments must be made
- **Total amount payable** — the full sum you will pay over the life of the agreement
- **Creditor details** — name and address of the lender
- **Duration** — the length of the agreement

If any of these terms are missing or incorrectly stated, the agreement may be unenforceable under Section 127.

### How to Challenge

1. Request a copy of your credit agreement from the creditor (you are entitled to this under Section 77/78)
2. Check every prescribed term against the Act
3. If terms are missing or wrong, write to the creditor arguing the agreement is unenforceable
4. If the creditor refuses, escalate to the **Financial Ombudsman Service**

## Time Orders: Breathing Space When You Cannot Pay

If you are struggling to make repayments, a **time order** can give you the breathing space you need.

A time order is a court order that:

- **Extends the repayment period** — spreading payments over a longer time
- **Reduces monthly payments** — making them affordable
- **Suspends enforcement action** — stopping the creditor from pursuing you while the order is in place

### How to Apply

- Apply through **Money Claim Online** or your local **county court**
- You must show you are unable to pay currently but may be able to pay in the future
- The court will consider your income, outgoings, and the total debt

Time orders are particularly useful for hire purchase and conditional sale agreements, but can apply to other regulated credit.

## Limitation Periods: How Long Can a Creditor Chase?

Creditors cannot chase debts forever. The law sets time limits for enforcement:

- **Simple contract debts** (most credit cards, loans): **6 years** from the date of default
- **Sealed orders** (county court judgments): **12 years** from the date of the order

After the limitation period expires, the debt still exists — but the creditor loses the right to take court action to enforce it. The debt becomes **statute-barred**.

### Important Notes

- Making a partial payment or acknowledging the debt in writing can restart the limitation clock
- The creditor can still contact you about a statute-barred debt, but cannot use the courts to force payment
- If you believe a debt is statute-barred, seek legal advice before responding to the creditor

## Consumer Rights Act 2015: Goods on Credit

If you buy goods using a credit card or through hire purchase, you also have rights under the **Consumer Rights Act 2015**:

- **Goods must be as described** — matching the description, sample, or model shown
- **Goods must be fit for purpose** — suitable for the use you told the seller
- **Goods must be of satisfactory quality** — free from defects and durable

### Key Rights

| Right | Timeframe |
|-------|-----------|
| **Right to reject** | Within 30 days for a full refund |
| **Right to repair or replacement** | Within 6 months — seller gets one attempt |
| **Right to price reduction or final rejection** | After failed repair/replacement or after 6 months (with deduction for use) |

If you paid more than £100 on a credit card for goods costing £100–£30,000, you may also have a **Section 75 claim** against the credit card provider. This makes the card issuer jointly liable with the seller.

## Hire Purchase: Your Specific Rights

Hire purchase (HP) agreements have extra protections:

- **You can terminate the agreement** after paying at least **50% of the total amount payable**
- When you terminate, the creditor must return **50% of the amount you have already paid**
- The goods belong to the creditor until you make the final payment
- You cannot sell the goods without the creditor's permission

### Before 50% Is Paid

If you default on payments before reaching the 50% mark, the creditor can repossess the goods — but must give you reasonable notice and obtain a court order if you have paid one-third or more of the total amount.

## Conditional Sale Agreements

Conditional sale is similar to hire purchase:

- Goods belong to the creditor until you have paid the full amount
- You can terminate after paying **50% of the total**
- The creditor must return 50% of what you have paid if you terminate
- Repossession rules are the same as hire purchase

## Worked Example: John's Credit Card Challenge

John has a credit card debt of **£5,000** with a high-interest lender. He requests a copy of his agreement under Section 77 and discovers that the prescribed terms are incomplete — the APR is missing and the repayment schedule is not clearly stated.

John writes to the creditor arguing the agreement is unenforceable under Section 127. The creditor initially pushes back, but John escalates the complaint to the **Financial Ombudsman Service**.

After investigation, the Ombudsman agrees the agreement has significant errors. The creditor is forced to:

- Reduce the outstanding balance from **£5,000 to £3,000**
- Offer a **12-month interest-free payment plan**
- Write off **£2,000** of the original debt

John saves **£2,000** simply by knowing his rights under the Consumer Credit Act.

## Tips to Protect Yourself

- **Keep copies of all credit agreements** — store them safely and check the terms
- **Request your agreement under Section 77/78** if you suspect errors
- **Check for prescribed terms** — missing terms could make the agreement unenforceable
- **Act quickly** — limitation periods and challenge windows have deadlines
- **Complain to the Financial Ombudsman** if your creditor refuses to engage
- **Seek legal advice** for complex cases — Citizens Advice, Law Centres, and solicitors can help
- **Do not ignore debt** — contact your creditor early and explore time orders or repayment plans
