UK Financial Wellbeing: Money & Mental Health

June 16, 2026
🏷️ financial-wellbeing 🏷️ mental-health 🏷️ debt-advice 🏷️ financial-stress 🏷️ money-and-mental-health 🏷️ wellbeing

Money and mental health are deeply connected. When your finances are under strain, your mental health often suffers — and when your mental health declines, managing money becomes harder. This cycle affects millions of people across the UK. This guide explains the link between money and mental health, how to recognise the signs of financial stress, where to get free support, and practical steps you can take to improve both your financial and mental wellbeing.

The Link Between Money and Mental Health

Financial problems are one of the leading causes of stress, anxiety, and depression in the UK. The pressure of managing debt, keeping up with bills, or not knowing how you will pay for essentials can feel overwhelming.

Key facts

  • Around 42% of UK adults report that money worries have negatively affected their mental health.
  • People in problem debt are twice as likely to experience depression compared to those without debt.
  • Financial stress is linked to sleep problems, relationship difficulties, reduced performance at work, and social isolation.
  • Money worries do not discriminate — they affect people across all income levels, ages, and backgrounds.

The relationship works both ways. Financial pressure can trigger or worsen mental health conditions, and mental health conditions can make it harder to manage money effectively. Breaking the cycle often requires addressing both issues together.

Signs of Financial Stress

Financial stress does not always look like missed bill payments or bailiff letters. It can show up in subtle ways that are easy to dismiss or overlook. Recognising the signs early is the first step toward getting help.

Common signs include

  • Avoiding financial reality: Not opening post, ignoring bank statements, or avoiding checking your online banking because you are afraid of what you might find.
  • Sleep disruption: Lying awake at night worrying about money, or finding that financial anxiety is the first thing on your mind when you wake up.
  • Relationship strain: Arguments about money with your partner, family, or housemates. Money is one of the most common sources of conflict in relationships.
  • Using credit for essentials: Relying on credit cards, overdrafts, or payday loans to cover everyday costs like food, rent, or utility bills.
  • Feeling hopeless: A sense that your financial situation will never improve, or feeling paralysed and unable to take action.
  • Physical symptoms: Headaches, stomach problems, chest tightness, or other physical symptoms caused by stress.
  • Withdrawal: Avoiding social situations because you cannot afford to participate, or isolating yourself because of shame about your financial situation.
  • Irritability: Feeling short-tempered, anxious, or on edge, particularly when money is mentioned.

If you recognise several of these signs, you are not alone — and there is support available.

  • Turn2Us helps people in financial hardship access grants, benefits, and other support.

Practical Steps to Improve Your Financial Wellbeing

Taking small, manageable steps can help you regain a sense of control. You do not need to solve everything at once.

1. Create a simple budget

You do not need complex spreadsheets. Start with a basic list of what comes in and what goes out. Identify the essentials (rent, food, utilities) and non-essentials. This gives you a clear picture of your financial situation.

2. Contact your creditors

If you are struggling to pay bills or repay debt, contact your creditors as soon as possible. Under FCA rules, creditors must treat you fairly and consider affordable repayment plans. Many lenders have dedicated support teams for customers in financial difficulty.

  • Explain your situation honestly.
  • Ask about reduced payments, payment holidays, or debt management options.
  • Get any agreement in writing.

3. Get free debt advice

Organisations like StepChange and National Debtline can review your situation and help you create a plan. They can negotiate with creditors on your behalf and help you understand options like Debt Management Plans, Debt Relief Orders, or, in some cases, bankruptcy.

4. Check your benefits entitlement

You may be entitled to benefits you are not claiming. Use the GOV.UK benefits calculator or contact Citizens Advice to check. Common overlooked benefits include Universal Credit, Council Tax Reduction, and New Style ESA.

5. Build an emergency fund

Even small savings can make a difference. Aim to build an emergency fund of three to six months of essential expenses. Start with whatever you can afford — even £5 or £10 a month adds up over time.

6. Prioritise essential bills

If you cannot pay all your bills, prioritise the essentials: rent or mortgage, council tax, energy bills, and food. These should come before credit card payments or other non-essential debts.

Mental Health and Money: How They Affect Each Other

Mental health conditions can directly impact your ability to manage money. Understanding this connection can help you be more compassionate with yourself and seek the right support.

Depression and money

Depression can cause a loss of motivation, making it difficult to open post, check accounts, or make decisions. Some people overspend as a way to cope with low mood, while others may stop spending entirely, missing essential payments.

Anxiety and money

Anxiety can lead to avoidance — avoiding checking bank balances, ignoring bills, or putting off financial decisions. This avoidance can make financial problems worse, which in turn increases anxiety.

Bipolar disorder and money

During manic or hypomanic episodes, impulsivity can lead to overspending, risky investments, or financial commitments that are difficult to sustain. During depressive episodes, the resulting debt can feel crushing.

ADHD and money

Difficulty with executive function, organisation, and impulse control can make budgeting, paying bills on time, and resisting impulse purchases particularly challenging.

What to do

  • Speak to your GP. Mental health conditions are treatable. Getting the right support — whether therapy, medication, or a combination — can improve your ability to manage money.
  • Tell your debt adviser. Organisations like StepChange have trained staff who understand the link between mental health and debt. They can adjust their support to your needs.
  • Request reasonable adjustments. Banks, creditors, and debt collectors must make reasonable adjustments for people with mental health conditions. This might include communicating in writing rather than by phone, allowing a trusted person to act on your behalf, or pausing enforcement action while you receive treatment.

Workplace Support

Your employer may be able to help. Many UK employers offer support programmes that can assist with both financial and mental health difficulties.

Employee Assistance Programme (EAP)

An EAP is a confidential service offered by many employers. It typically provides:

  • Free counselling sessions (usually 3 to 6 sessions)
  • Financial advice and guidance
  • Legal information
  • Stress management support

Check with your HR department or employee handbook to see if your employer offers an EAP. The service is usually completely free and confidential — your employer will not be told you have used it.

Other workplace support

  • Salary advance schemes: Some employers allow you to access earned wages before payday.
  • Flexible working: If your mental health is affecting your ability to work, your employer may be able to offer flexible hours or adjustments.
  • Occupational health: Your employer may refer you to an occupational health service for support.

Financial Abuse: When Money Is Used as Control

If someone is using money to control you, this is financial abuse. It is a form of domestic abuse and is recognised as a criminal offence in England and Wales.

Signs of financial abuse include:

  • Being prevented from working or earning your own money.
  • Having your wages taken or redirected.
  • Being given a strict “allowance” and monitored on every purchase.
  • Having debt taken out in your name without your consent.

If you are experiencing financial abuse, see our financial abuse guide for detailed support and contact information. You can also contact the National Domestic Abuse Helpline on 0808 2000 247.

Worked Example: Lisa’s Journey to Financial and Mental Wellbeing

Lisa is 28 and works in retail. She has £8,000 in credit card debt spread across three cards. She has been making minimum payments for over a year and feels overwhelmed. She has stopped opening her post because the letters from creditors make her anxious. She has been losing sleep and feeling irritable with her partner.

What Lisa does

Step 1: Lisa contacts StepChange on 0800 138 1111. A debt adviser reviews her income and outgoings and confirms she is eligible for a Debt Management Plan (DMP).

Step 2: Under the DMP, Lisa makes a single monthly payment of £150, which is distributed to her creditors. Her creditors agree to freeze interest and charges while she is on the plan.

Step 3: Lisa also visits her GP about her anxiety. She is referred for Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT), which helps her develop coping strategies for her anxiety, including around money.

Step 4: Lisa’s employer offers an EAP, and she uses the free counselling sessions to talk through the stress she has been experiencing.

Results after 12 months

  • Lisa’s debt is being reduced steadily. At £150 per month, her £8,000 debt will be cleared in approximately 5 years.
  • Her anxiety has improved with CBT. She now opens her post promptly and reviews her DMP statements.
  • She and her partner have fewer arguments about money because the situation is under control.
  • Lisa has started building a small emergency fund of £500.

Key takeaway

Lisa’s situation improved because she addressed both the financial problem and her mental health at the same time. Neither issue needed to be fully resolved before she could start making progress on the other.

Tips for Improving Your Financial and Mental Wellbeing

  • You are not alone. Millions of people in the UK are struggling with money and mental health. There is no shame in asking for help.
  • Talk to someone. Whether it is a friend, family member, debt adviser, or counsellor — talking about your worries can reduce their power.
  • Free debt advice exists. Organisations like StepChange, National Debtline, and Citizens Advice provide expert support at no cost.
  • Contact creditors early. The sooner you tell your creditor you are struggling, the more options they can offer. Ignoring the problem makes it worse.
  • Prioritise your mental health. Financial problems are easier to solve when you are in a better mental state. Speak to your GP about any anxiety, depression, or stress you are experiencing.
  • Small steps count. You do not need to fix everything overnight. Even making one phone call or opening one letter is progress.
  • Be kind to yourself. Financial difficulty is not a moral failing. Life events like job loss, illness, relationship breakdown, or the cost of living crisis can affect anyone.
