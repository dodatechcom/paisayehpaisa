UK Employer Benefits: Get More Than Just Salary

June 16, 2026
🏷️ employer-benefits 🏷️ workplace-pension 🏷️ private-medical-insurance 🏷️ salary-sacrifice 🏷️ compensation 🏷️ personal-finance

Your salary is only part of what your employer pays you. UK workers often overlook benefits worth thousands of pounds each year, simply because they do not know they exist or do not use them. Understanding your employer benefits package can add £3,000–£10,000 or more to your total compensation — tax-efficiently.

This guide covers the most common UK employer benefits, what they are worth, and how to make sure you are getting everything you are entitled to.

Pension: Your Most Valuable Benefit

Your workplace pension is almost certainly the most valuable benefit your employer offers. Since auto-enrolment was introduced in 2012, every UK employer must provide a pension scheme and contribute a minimum of 3% of your qualifying earnings.

How Workplace Pensions Work

  1. You contribute from your salary (typically 5%)
  2. Your employer contributes at least 3%
  3. Both contributions receive tax relief
  4. The combined pot grows over decades through investment returns

Employer Matching

Many employers contribute more than the statutory minimum. Some match your contributions up to a higher percentage — for example, if you contribute 6%, they contribute 6%. This is free money. If your employer offers matching above the minimum, contribute at least enough to get the full match.

Example

Your ContributionEmployer ContributionAnnual Total (on £35,000 salary)
5% (statutory min)3% (statutory min)£2,800
6% (matched)6% (matched)£4,200

The difference between 5% and 6% contributions costs you £350 per year but adds £1,400 to your pension pot — a 4x return before investment growth.

What to Check

  • What percentage does your employer contribute?
  • Is there a matching scheme above the minimum?
  • What is the pension scheme name and provider?
  • Are there salary sacrifice options for pension contributions?
  • What are the default investment funds?

Private Medical Insurance (PMI)

Many UK employers provide private medical insurance as a benefit. This gives you access to private healthcare alongside the NHS, reducing waiting times and expanding your treatment options.

What PMI Typically Covers

  • Inpatient treatment — surgery, hospital stays, diagnostics
  • Outpatient treatment — consultant appointments, scans, physiotherapy
  • Mental health — counselling, therapy, psychiatric care
  • Dental — some policies include dental treatment
  • Optical — eye tests and corrective lenses (less common)

What to Check

  • Does your employer offer PMI? Many do not advertise it clearly.
  • Is it individual cover or family cover?
  • Are pre-existing conditions excluded? (They often are.)
  • Is there a excess you need to pay?
  • Can you use the NHS and claim through PMI for faster treatment?

Value

A basic PMI policy for an individual typically costs £500–£1,500 per year depending on age and coverage level. If your employer provides it, that is a significant benefit with no cost to you.

Life Insurance: Death-in-Service Benefit

Many UK employers provide a life insurance benefit known as “death-in-service” cover. If you die while employed, your nominated beneficiaries receive a tax-free lump sum.

Typical Coverage

Most employers provide cover of 2–4 times your annual salary. For example, on a £40,000 salary with 3x cover, your beneficiaries would receive £120,000 tax-free.

What to Check

  • How many times your salary does the policy cover?
  • Who are your nominated beneficiaries? Update this if your circumstances change.
  • Does the cover continue if you leave the employer? (Usually not.)
  • Is there a separate group life policy you can review?

Value

A comparable standalone life insurance policy for a healthy 30-year-old might cost £10–£20 per month. Employer-provided cover is typically free to you and more generous than what you would buy individually.

Income Protection

Some employers provide income protection beyond the statutory sick pay minimum. This means if you are unable to work due to illness or injury, your employer continues to pay your salary for a defined period.

Statutory vs Employer Sick Pay

TypePaymentDuration
Statutory Sick Pay (SSP)£116.75 per week (2025/26)Up to 28 weeks
Employer sick payVaries — often full salaryVaries by employer

What to Check

  • Does your employer offer enhanced sick pay beyond SSP?
  • For how long is full salary paid during sickness?
  • Is there an occupational health process?
  • Does the policy cover long-term illness?

Share Schemes

UK employers may offer share schemes that allow you to buy company shares at a discount, tax-efficiently.

Share Incentive Plan (SIP)

A SIP allows you to buy shares in your employer’s company using your pre-tax salary. Your employer may also match your contributions — for example, for every share you buy, they give you one free.

  • Tax-free — no income tax or capital gains tax if you hold shares for the qualifying period (typically 3–5 years)
  • Employer matching — free shares on top of your own
  • Flexible — buy up to £3,600 of shares per year

Save As You Earn (SAYE)

SAYE is a savings scheme where you save a fixed amount each month for 3 or 5 years. At the end, you can use your savings to buy shares at a discounted price — typically at least 20% below market value.

  • Tax-free — no income tax on the discount
  • No risk — if the share price has fallen, you can walk away and keep your savings
  • Guaranteed discount — the price is fixed when you start

What to Check

  • Does your employer offer a SIP or SAYE scheme?
  • What matching or bonus shares are available?
  • What is the qualifying period?
  • Is the scheme open to all employees?

Cycle to Work

The Cycle to Work scheme lets you buy a bike and cycling equipment tax-free through your employer. You save income tax and National Insurance on the amount — typically 28–42% depending on your tax rate.

How It Works

  1. Your employer buys the bike and equipment on your behalf
  2. You “rent” the bike from your employer through salary sacrifice
  3. At the end of the agreement (typically 12 months), you buy the bike at a fair market value
  4. You have saved 28–42% on the original price

Limits

There is no official limit on the scheme, but many employers set a cap of £1,000–£3,000 for the bike and equipment.

Value

A £1,000 bike through the scheme might cost you £600–£720 after tax savings, depending on your marginal rate. If you use the bike for commuting, you also save on train fares, petrol, or parking.

Electric Car Scheme (Salary Sacrifice)

Salary sacrifice for electric cars has become one of the most generous UK employer benefits in recent years. You give up part of your gross salary in exchange for an electric car, with significant tax advantages.

Why Electric Cars Are So Tax-Efficient

Electric cars have a Benefit in Kind (BIK) rate of just 2% for 2025/26. Compare this to petrol cars, which can attract BIK rates of 20–37%. This means the tax cost of a company electric car is minimal.

How It Works

  1. You agree a salary sacrifice amount with your employer
  2. Your employer leases the electric car for you
  3. Your gross salary is reduced, saving income tax and National Insurance
  4. You pay a small amount of BIK tax (typically £10–£30 per month for a mid-range EV)

Example

A Tesla Model 3 costs around £450 per month on lease. Through salary sacrifice:

ItemMonthly Cost
Gross salary sacrifice£450
Income tax saved (20%)£90
NI saved (8%)£36
BIK tax (2%)£12
Net monthly cost£312

You drive a £45,000 car for £312 per month, fully insured and maintained.

What to Check

  • Does your employer offer an electric car salary sacrifice scheme?
  • What is the lease term? (Typically 3–4 years.)
  • Are insurance, maintenance, and breakdown cover included?
  • What happens when you leave the company?

Flexible Working

Since April 2024, all UK employees have the right to request flexible working from day one of employment. Some employers go further, offering structured flexible working policies as a formal benefit.

Types of Flexible Working

  • Remote working — working from home full-time or part-time
  • Hybrid working — split between office and home
  • Compressed hours — full-time hours over fewer days
  • Flexitime — flexible start and finish times
  • Job sharing — two people sharing one role

Value

Flexible working has no direct monetary value, but the savings on commuting, childcare, and work clothing can be substantial. London commuters can save £3,000–£5,000 per year by working from home two or three days per week.

Gym Membership

Some employers provide discounted or free gym memberships, either through a corporate scheme or on-site facilities.

What to Check

  • Does your employer offer a gym membership benefit?
  • Is it a corporate discount or fully subsidised?
  • Can you use it at any gym or only specific chains?
  • Is the benefit available to all employees or only at certain levels?

Value

A gym membership typically costs £30–£80 per month. A corporate discount of 20–50% saves you £100–£400 per year.

Training and Professional Development

Many employers offer a training budget for professional development. This can cover certifications, courses, conferences, and degree programmes.

What to Check

  • Is there a defined training budget per employee per year?
  • What approvals are needed?
  • Can the budget be used for qualifications outside your immediate role?
  • Does the employer support part-time study or degree programmes?

Value

Professional certifications can cost £500–£5,000. Employer-funded training is a direct investment in your career with no cost to you. Use it — employees who do not use their training budget leave money and career development on the table.

Worked Example: Employee Benefits Value

Consider Priya, who earns £35,000 per year as a marketing manager. Her employer provides the following benefits:

BenefitEmployer ContributionEstimated Value
Pension (5% employer contribution)£1,750 per year£1,750
Private medical insurance£500 per year£500
Life insurance (2x salary)£70,000 cover£200
Cycle to Work scheme£500 bike£150 (tax saving)
Gym membership discount£30/month (50% off)£180 per year
Training budget£1,000 per year£1,000
Total£3,780 per year

Priya’s total compensation is actually £38,780, not £35,000. If she ignores these benefits, she undervalues her package by nearly £4,000.

Tips for Maximising Your Benefits

  • Check all benefits at interview. Ask about the full benefits package before accepting a job offer. Do not assume salary is the only negotiation point.
  • Negotiate benefits, not just salary. If an employer cannot move on salary, they may be able to add an extra holiday day, increase pension contributions, or provide PMI.
  • Use all benefits available. Many employees do not use benefits they are entitled to. Review your employment contract and HR portal.
  • Review benefits annually. Benefits change. Check each year for new offerings or adjustments to existing schemes.
  • Ask about new benefits. If your employer does not offer something you want, ask. Many companies introduce benefits based on employee feedback.
  • Understand tax implications. Some benefits are tax-free, others attract BIK tax. Know the difference so you are not caught out.
