How to Transfer a Pension: Consolidate Your Retirement Savings

Over time, it is easy to accumulate multiple pension pots from different jobs. Transferring and consolidating them can make your retirement savings easier to manage and potentially reduce fees. But it is not always the right move. This guide explains when to transfer, when not to, and how to do it correctly.

Why Consider a Pension Transfer?

The most common reason is consolidation — bringing multiple small pensions together so you can see everything in one place and potentially benefit from lower fees.

Common Reasons to Transfer

ReasonBenefit
Lost pensionsFind and reconnect with forgotten pots
Lower feesSave thousands over your lifetime
Better investment optionsAccess to wider range of funds
Easier managementOne login, one statement
Better customer serviceModern platforms with better support
Tax-free cash maximisationSome schemes offer better tax-free cash terms

Finding Lost Pensions

The UK has an estimated £31 billion in lost or forgotten pensions. The Pension Tracing Service helps you find them.

How to Trace a Lost Pension

StepAction
1. Gather informationEmployer names, dates of employment, scheme names
2. Use the Pension Tracing ServiceFree service at gov.uk/pension-tracing-service
3. Contact old employersHR departments may have records
4. Check the Pension Protection FundFor schemes that have failed
5. Use the government’s free guidancePension Wise appointment

Worked example: You worked at three different companies between 2005 and 2015. You have pension pots at each but do not know the values. You use the Pension Tracing Service to find the scheme details, then contact each provider. You discover you have £15,000, £8,000, and £12,000 — a total of £35,000 you did not know about.

The Transfer Process

Step 1: Get a Valuation

Contact your current pension provider(s) to get:

  • Current value of your pension
  • Any exit fees or penalties
  • Tax-free cash entitlement
  • Current investment performance
  • Ongoing charges

Step 2: Compare Options

Compare your current pension with the new provider:

FactorCurrent PensionNew Pension
Annual management charge0.75%0.25%
Investment options10 funds100+ funds
Customer servicePhone onlyApp + online
Exit fees£0£0
Tax-free cash25%25%

Step 3: Apply to Transfer

Contact the new provider and:

  • Complete a transfer application
  • Provide details of your old pension
  • Authorise the transfer
  • Choose your investment strategy

Step 4: Wait for Completion

Transfer TypeTimeline
Defined contribution to defined contribution4-8 weeks
Defined benefit to defined contribution3-6 months
Small pension (under £10,000)2-4 weeks
Complex transfer6-12 months

Step 5: Verify the Transfer

Check that:

  • The full value has been transferred
  • No unexpected fees were deducted
  • The money is invested as you requested
  • Your tax-free cash entitlement is preserved

Defined Benefit vs Defined Contribution

Understanding the difference is crucial before transferring.

Defined Benefit (Final Salary) Pensions

FeatureDetail
GuaranteeGuaranteed income for life
ValueOften worth 20-30x the transfer value
RiskLow — employer bears investment risk
TransferUsually inadvisable unless specific circumstances

Defined Contribution (Money Purchase) Pensions

FeatureDetail
GuaranteeNo guaranteed income
ValueDepends on contributions and investment performance
RiskHigher — you bear investment risk
TransferUsually straightforward

Critical warning: Transferring a defined benefit pension is almost always a bad idea unless you have specific circumstances (short life expectancy, severe health issues, or very large pension pots). The guaranteed income is extremely valuable.

Worked example: You have a defined benefit pension offering £10,000/year for life from age 65. The transfer value is £250,000. If you invest this at 4% growth and draw 4% per year, you would get £10,000/year. But the defined benefit pension is guaranteed for life, while the invested pot could run out if you live longer than expected or if investment returns are poor.

When a defined benefit transfer might be considered:

  • You have a very short life expectancy
  • You have a very large pension pot (over £1 million)
  • You have other guaranteed income sources
  • You have received regulated financial advice confirming it is in your best interest

Important: Since 2015, anyone with a defined benefit pension worth over £30,000 must receive independent financial advice before transferring.

Exit Fees and Charges

Some pensions charge exit fees if you transfer before a certain age.

Common Exit Fee Structures

Fee TypeTypical RangeWhen Applied
Market value adjustment (MVA)0-20%With-profits pensions
Exit penalty0-10%Early retirement pensions
Transfer fee£0-500Administrative charge
Ongoing charge reductionVariesSome schemes waive fees on exit

Worked example: You have a with-profits pension with a transfer value of £50,000. The provider applies a market value adjustment of 10% because you are transferring before age 55. You lose £5,000. If you wait until age 55, the MVA may not apply.

Tip: Always ask for the exact transfer value including any adjustments before deciding to transfer.

Protected Tax-Free Cash

Some older pensions allow you to take more than 25% tax-free cash. If you transfer, you may lose this protection.

Protected Tax-Free Cash Rules

SituationProtected Amount
Pre-April 2006 pensionUp to 100% tax-free cash possible
Fixed protection 2012Up to £1.5 million tax-free
Individual protection 2014Up to £1.25 million tax-free
Standard25% of pension pot

Critical warning: If you have protected tax-free cash and transfer to a new provider, you may lose this protection permanently. Check with your current provider before transferring.

Worked example: You have a pre-2006 pension with £100,000 and 50% tax-free cash protection. You can take £50,000 tax-free. If you transfer to a modern pension, you lose this protection and can only take £25,000 tax-free. You have just lost £25,000 in tax-free benefits.

When NOT to Transfer

There are several situations where transferring is almost always wrong.

When to Keep Your Pension Where It Is

SituationWhy Not to Transfer
Defined benefit pensionGuaranteed income is too valuable
Protected tax-free cashYou will lose the protection
High transfer feesFees eat into your pension value
With-profits pension near maturityBonuses are about to be added
Employer contribution matchingYou would lose free money
Insured benefitsGuaranteed annuity rates, etc.

Worked example: You have a defined benefit pension from your civil service job. It offers £12,000/year for life from age 60 with inflation protection. The transfer value is £300,000. Transferring would give you £300,000 to invest, but you lose the guaranteed income. Unless you are an expert investor with a long time horizon, this is almost certainly a bad trade.

Getting Financial Advice

For large pension pots or complex situations, professional advice is essential.

When You Need Advice

SituationWhy Advice Is Needed
Defined benefit pensionLegally required if over £30,000
Multiple pensionsComplex comparison needed
Protected tax-free cashRisk of losing protection
Large pension potSignificant consequences of wrong decision
Health issuesMay affect transfer value
Inheritance planningTax implications for beneficiaries

How to Find a Financial Adviser

  • Unbiased.co.uk — Independent financial advisers
  • VouchedFor.co.uk — Verified advisers
  • Pension Wise — Free government guidance (not advice)
  • The Pensions Advisory Service — Free guidance

Cost: Financial advisers typically charge 1-2% of the pension value for a transfer recommendation, or an hourly rate of £150-£300.

Worked example: You have a £200,000 defined benefit pension. You see a financial adviser who charges £3,000 for a full analysis. They recommend you do NOT transfer because the guaranteed income is worth more than the investment alternatives. The £3,000 advice fee saved you potentially hundreds of thousands in lost income.

Consolidation Checklist

If you decide to consolidate, use this checklist:

  • Found all pension pots (Pension Tracing Service)
  • Got valuations from all providers
  • Checked for exit fees on each pension
  • Checked for protected tax-free cash
  • Compared fees between current and new providers
  • Considered defined benefit vs defined contribution risks
  • Received financial advice if needed
  • Understood the transfer timeline
  • Verified the transfer after completion

The Bottom Line

Transferring a pension can save you thousands in fees and make your retirement savings easier to manage. But it is not always the right move. Never transfer a defined benefit pension without professional advice. Always check for exit fees, protected tax-free cash, and other benefits before making a decision. Use the Pension Tracing Service to find lost pots, compare fees carefully, and consider getting independent financial advice for complex situations.
