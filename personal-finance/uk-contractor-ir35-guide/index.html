UK IR35 & Contractor Tax: Off-Payroll Working Rules

June 16, 2026
🏷️ ir35 🏷️ contractor-tax 🏷️ self-employment 🏷️ off-payroll-working 🏷️ limited-company 🏷️ umbrella-company

IR35 is one of the most important — and misunderstood — areas of UK tax law for contractors. Whether you work through a limited company or an umbrella company, getting your IR35 status wrong can cost you thousands in extra tax. This guide explains how IR35 works, who determines your status, and how to structure your contracting to minimise your tax bill.

What Is IR35?

IR35 refers to the off-payroll working rules introduced by HMRC in 2000. The rules are designed to determine whether a contractor is genuinely self-employed or is actually a “disguised employee” — someone who works like an employee but invoices through a company to pay less tax.

The name “IR35” comes from the original Inland Revenue press release (IR35) that announced the legislation.

Why does IR35 exist?

Before IR35, contractors could set up a limited company and pay significantly less tax than an employee doing the same job. HMRC argued that some of these arrangements were not genuine — the contractor had no real control over how they worked, could not send a substitute, and was effectively an employee who happened to invoice through a company.

IR35 aims to close this gap by treating “disguised employees” as employees for tax purposes, ensuring they pay the same tax and National Insurance contributions as regular employees.

Who Determines Your IR35 Status?

Since April 2021, the responsibility for determining IR35 status depends on the size of the client.

Medium and large clients (since April 2021)

If your client is a medium or large company (meeting at least two of the following criteria: turnover more than £10.2 million, balance sheet total more than £5.1 million, or more than 50 employees), the client is responsible for determining whether your engagement falls inside or outside IR35.

The client must issue a Status Determination Statement (SDS) that sets out their determination and the reasons for it. They must take reasonable care when making this decision and cannot simply default to “inside IR35.”

Small companies

If your client is a small company (failing at least two of the three criteria above), you determine your own IR35 status. You are responsible for assessing whether your engagement is inside or outside IR35 and paying the correct tax accordingly.

Public sector

The off-payroll rules were introduced for the public sector in April 2017. The public sector body (the client) is always responsible for determining status, regardless of its size.

Inside IR35: How It Works

If your engagement falls inside IR35, HMRC considers you to be a disguised employee. This has significant financial implications.

What happens inside IR35

  • You are treated as an employee for tax purposes.
  • Your fee payer (the agency or client) must deduct PAYE income tax and National Insurance contributions from your payments.
  • Your fee payer must also pay employer National Insurance contributions (13.8% on earnings above the secondary threshold).
  • You cannot claim business expenses (in most cases).
  • You receive less take-home pay because of the additional tax and NI deductions.

Financial impact

Inside IR35, you lose the tax advantages of working through a limited company. Your take-home pay is similar to — or sometimes less than — that of a permanent employee on the same salary, because you do not get benefits like sick pay, holiday pay, or pension contributions.

Outside IR35: How It Works

If your engagement falls outside IR35, HMRC recognises that you are genuinely self-employed. You can operate through your own limited company and enjoy the tax advantages of doing so.

What happens outside IR35

  • You are treated as self-employed for tax purposes.
  • Your limited company invoices the client and receives payment.
  • You pay Corporation Tax on profits (currently 19% to 25% depending on profit level).
  • You can extract profits as dividends, which are subject to dividend tax but not National Insurance.
  • You can claim legitimate business expenses to reduce your Corporation Tax bill.
  • You have more flexibility over when and how you pay yourself.

Key requirement: business on own account

To operate outside IR35, you must demonstrate that you are in business on your own account. This means you are not just an employee who happens to invoice through a company — you have genuine autonomy, risk, and control over how you deliver your work.

IR35 Status Factors

HMRC and the courts consider several factors when determining whether an engagement is inside or outside IR35. No single factor is decisive — it is the overall picture that matters.

Control

  • Can the client dictate how, when, and where you work?
  • Do you set your own hours and work schedule?
  • Do you decide how to complete the work, or does the client tell you exactly what to do?

Outside IR35: You have significant control over how you deliver the work. You decide your methods, schedule, and approach.

Inside IR35: The client controls your working methods, hours, and location in the same way they would an employee.

Substitution

  • Can you send someone else to do the work in your place?
  • Do you have the right to provide a substitute?
  • Have you ever sent a substitute?

Outside IR35: You have an unrestricted right of substitution and have exercised it (or could exercise it in practice).

Inside IR35: You are expected to perform the work personally and cannot send a substitute.

Mutuality of obligation

  • Is the client obliged to offer you work?
  • Are you obliged to accept it?

Outside IR35: There is no ongoing obligation. Each assignment is a separate contract. The client can choose not to offer more work, and you can choose not to accept it.

Inside IR35: There is an expectation of ongoing work, similar to an employment relationship.

Financial risk

  • Do you bear financial risk? For example, if the work is done badly, do you have to redo it at your own cost?
  • Do you have your own insurance against professional risk?
  • Are you liable for defective work?

Outside IR35: You bear genuine financial risk. You have professional indemnity insurance and are liable for the quality of your work.

Inside IR35: The client bears the financial risk. You are paid for your time regardless of the quality of the output.

Equipment and resources

  • Do you provide your own laptop, phone, and tools?
  • Do you use your own software and licences?
  • Do you work from your own premises?

Outside IR35: You supply your own equipment and resources.

Inside IR35: The client provides your equipment, as they would for an employee.

Part and parcel

  • Are you integrated into the client’s organisation?
  • Do you attend company meetings, social events, or training sessions?
  • Do you have a client email address or badge?

Outside IR35: You operate independently. You are not absorbed into the client’s organisation.

Inside IR35: You are treated as part of the team, indistinguishable from employees.

Umbrella Company vs Limited Company

If you work as a contractor, you generally have two options for how to operate.

Umbrella company

An umbrella company employs you. The umbrella invoices the client or agency on your behalf. You receive a salary after the umbrella deducts:

  • Income tax (PAYE)
  • Employee National Insurance contributions (12% on earnings between the primary threshold and upper earnings limit, 2% above that)
  • The umbrella company’s margin (typically £50 to £100 per month)

Advantages of an umbrella company:

  • Simple administration — no need to manage a limited company.
  • You are employed and accrue employment rights (statutory sick pay, holiday pay, pension auto-enrolment).
  • No risk of IR35 determination — you are already treated as an employee.
  • Useful for short-term contracts or if you are unsure about your IR35 status.

Disadvantages of an umbrella company:

  • Higher tax burden — you pay more tax and NI than through a limited company.
  • The umbrella margin reduces your take-home pay.
  • Less control over your tax affairs.

Limited company

A limited company is your own company. You are a director and shareholder. The company invoices the client, receives payment, and you pay yourself through a combination of salary and dividends.

Advantages of a limited company:

  • Most tax-efficient structure for contractors operating outside IR35.
  • You can claim legitimate business expenses to reduce Corporation Tax.
  • You can time dividend payments to manage your tax liability.
  • Dividends are not subject to National Insurance.
  • Greater control over your finances.

Disadvantages of a limited company:

  • More administration — you must file accounts with Companies House and file a Corporation Tax return.
  • You are responsible for your own IR35 status determination (for small clients).
  • You have no employment rights as a director.
  • You need an accountant to manage the tax and compliance.

Which is better?

For most contractors working outside IR35, a limited company is the most tax-efficient option. An umbrella company is simpler but costs you more in tax. If you are working inside IR35, an umbrella company is often the practical choice because the tax advantage of a limited company is removed.

Allowable Expenses

If you operate through a limited company and work outside IR35, you can claim expenses that are “wholly and exclusively” for the purpose of your business. This reduces your Corporation Tax bill.

What you cannot claim

  • Private expenses that are not related to your business
  • Entertainment expenses (with limited exceptions)
  • Clothing (unless it is a uniform or protective clothing)
  • Fines or penalties
  • Costs that have already been reimbursed by the client

Consider an IT contractor with a day rate of £500 working 220 days per year. Revenue: £110,000.

The difference

The difference in take-home pay between the two arrangements is approximately £19,250 per year — almost £1,600 per month. This is why getting your IR35 status right matters so much.

Note: The inside IR35 figure includes employer NI, which in practice is often passed to the client or factored into the rate. If the client absorbs employer NI, the contractor’s net take-home would be higher — but still significantly lower than outside IR35.

Tips for Contractors

  • Get a status determination from your client. If your client is a medium or large company, they must provide you with a Status Determination Statement. Check it carefully and challenge it if you believe it is wrong.
  • Keep records of your working arrangements. Evidence of substitution, control, and financial risk helps demonstrate your status. Keep contracts, emails, and invoices.
  • Claim all allowable expenses. Every legitimate expense reduces your Corporation Tax bill. Work with your accountant to identify everything you can claim.
  • Consider a limited company. For most contractors outside IR35, a limited company is the most tax-efficient structure. Set one up through Companies House or use an formation agent.
  • Get a good accountant. A specialist contractor accountant can save you money on tax, handle your accounts and tax returns, and advise on IR35. Expect to pay £100 to £200 per month.
  • Review your status annually. Your IR35 status can change if your working arrangements change. Review it every year and at each new contract.
  • Don’t ignore IR35. HMRC has increased its compliance activity and can look back several years. Getting it wrong can result in backdated tax bills, interest, and penalties.
  • Use HMRC’s CEST tool. HMRC provides the Check Employment Status for Tax (CEST) tool at gov.uk. While not legally binding, it can help you understand your status.
