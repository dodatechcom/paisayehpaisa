UK Self-Employed: Beyond Tax — Running Your Business Finances

June 16, 2026
🏷️ self-employed 🏷️ freelancing 🏷️ business-finance 🏷️ invoicing 🏷️ cash-flow 🏷️ vat 🏷️ corporation-tax 🏷️ limited-company 🏷️ sole-trader 🏷️ insurance

Being self-employed in the UK gives you freedom and control. It also means you are responsible for every aspect of your finances — not just tax. This guide goes beyond the basics of Self Assessment to cover the practical financial tasks that keep your business running smoothly.

Business Bank Account: Separate Your Money

A business bank account is not legally required if you are a sole trader. But it is strongly recommended. Mixing personal and business finances creates confusion, makes bookkeeping harder, and can cause problems with HMRC.

Why Separate Your Accounts

  • Clarity — you can see exactly what your business is earning and spending
  • Accounting — bookkeeping and tax returns become simpler
  • Professionalism — clients expect to pay into a business account
  • Liability — if you are a limited company, you are legally required to have a separate business bank account
  • Tax efficiency — clearer records mean fewer missed expenses

Invoicing: Get Paid Properly

Professional invoicing is not just about looking polished — it ensures you get paid on time and have clear records for tax.

What Your Invoice Must Include

For HMRC compliance, your invoice should contain:

  • Your name (or business name if registered)
  • Your address
  • Your client’s name and address
  • A unique invoice number
  • The date of issue
  • The date of payment
  • A description of the goods or services provided
  • The amount owed (excluding VAT if you are not VAT-registered)
  • VAT amount and VAT registration number (if applicable)
  • Your bank details for payment
  • Payment terms (e.g., 14 days, 30 days)

Invoice Software

Using invoicing software saves time and reduces errors:

  • FreeAgent — designed for UK freelancers and small businesses. Handles invoicing, expenses, tax estimates, and Self Assessment. Starts at £12/month (often free through NatWest/RBS business accounts)
  • Wave — free invoicing and accounting for small businesses. Good for start-ups with simple needs
  • Xero — popular with accountants. Starts at £15/month
  • QuickBooks — widely used, integrates with most UK banks. Starts at £12/month

Tips for Getting Paid on Time

  • Send invoices immediately after completing work
  • Set clear payment terms (14 days is standard for freelancers)
  • Include a polite reminder about late payment charges (you are entitled to statutory interest under the Late Payment of Commercial Debts Act 1998)
  • Follow up promptly if payment is late — send a reminder email at 7 days, then call at 14 days
  • Consider requesting upfront deposits (25–50%) for larger projects

Cash Flow: The Number One Priority

Running out of cash is the most common cause of business failure in the UK. You can be profitable on paper and still go bust if clients pay late and you cannot cover your bills.

Track Money In and Out

Keep a running record of:

  • Money expected (invoices sent but not yet paid)
  • Money received (payments in your account)
  • Money owed (bills and expenses to pay)
  • Money available (what you actually have right now)

Build a Cash Buffer

Keep at least 3 months’ essential expenses in your business account. For most self-employed people, this means:

  • Rent or mortgage payments
  • Council tax
  • Utilities
  • Insurance premiums
  • Loan repayments
  • Tax set-aside (see below)

Set Aside Money for Tax

A common mistake is spending all your income and then being hit with a tax bill. Set aside 25–30% of every payment you receive into a separate savings account. Use this account exclusively for tax payments — Corporation Tax, Income Tax, and National Insurance.

Cash Flow Forecasting

Create a simple 12-month cash flow forecast. List your expected income month by month, then subtract your known expenses. This shows you when you might be short and allows you to plan — for example, by arranging an overdraft or adjusting spending.

Insurance: Protect Your Business

Insurance is not legally required for most self-employed people (except employer’s liability if you have staff). But operating without it is a significant risk.

Essential Insurance Types

Public Liability Insurance

If clients visit your office or you visit client premises, public liability insurance covers you if someone is injured or their property is damaged because of your business activities. Typical cover: £1 million to £10 million. Cost: £50–£200 per year for most freelancers.

Professional Indemnity Insurance

If you provide advice, designs, or professional services, professional indemnity insurance covers you if a client claims your work caused them financial loss. This is essential for consultants, designers, developers, and advisers. Typical cover: £1 million to £5 million. Cost: £100–£400 per year depending on your profession and turnover.

Contents Insurance

If you work from home or rent office space, contents insurance covers your equipment — laptop, phone, monitor, desk, chair, and any other business equipment. Check whether your home insurance covers business equipment. Many policies limit cover to £250–£500 for business items, which may not be enough.

Employer’s Liability Insurance

If you hire anyone — even part-time or on a casual basis — you must have employer’s liability insurance by law. Minimum cover: £5 million. Without it, you face fines of up to £2,500 per day.

What to Consider

  • Check whether your client contracts require specific insurance types or levels
  • Review policies annually as your business grows
  • Consider cyber insurance if you handle client data
  • Some professional bodies require members to hold specific insurance

Contracts: Always Get It in Writing

Verbal agreements are legally binding in the UK, but proving what was agreed is extremely difficult. Always use written contracts.

Essential Contract Terms

Your contract should include:

  • Scope of work — exactly what you will deliver
  • Payment terms — amount, schedule, and method
  • Timeline — deadlines and milestones
  • Cancellation clause — how either party can end the agreement
  • Liability limitations — what you are and are not responsible for
  • Intellectual property ownership — who owns the work product
  • Confidentiality — protection of sensitive information
  • Dispute resolution — how disagreements will be handled

Where to Find Contract Templates

  • IPSE (Association of Independent Professionals and the Self-Employed) — provides contract templates for members
  • Lawbite — affordable legal templates for small businesses
  • Free legal templates — gov.uk and Citizens Advice offer basic templates
  • Your accountant or solicitor — for bespoke contracts tailored to your industry

Red Flags in Contracts

Watch out for:

  • “Work for hire” clauses that transfer all IP to the client without additional payment
  • Unlimited liability provisions
  • Payment terms longer than 30 days
  • Cancellation clauses that allow the client to terminate without paying for work completed
  • Indemnity clauses that make you liable for the client’s losses

VAT: When You Must Register

Value Added Tax (VAT) is charged on most goods and services in the UK. You must register for VAT if your taxable turnover exceeds the threshold.

VAT Registration Threshold

The VAT registration threshold is £90,000 for the 2024/25 tax year. You must register if:

  • Your VAT turnover exceeds £90,000 in any rolling 12-month period, or
  • You expect your turnover to exceed £90,000 in the next 30 days alone

Once registered, you must charge VAT on your sales, file VAT returns (usually quarterly), and pay the VAT you collect to HMRC (minus any VAT you have paid on business purchases).

Voluntary Registration

You can register for VAT even if your turnover is below the threshold. This is beneficial if:

  • Your clients are VAT-registered businesses (they can reclaim the VAT you charge, so it does not cost them extra)
  • You make zero-rated or exempt supplies (you can reclaim VAT on purchases but do not charge VAT on sales)
  • You want to appear more established (some clients prefer dealing with VAT-registered businesses)

VAT Schemes for Small Businesses

Flat Rate Scheme (FRS)

Instead of calculating VAT on each transaction, you pay a fixed percentage of your gross turnover. The rate depends on your industry (typically 10–16%). This simplifies accounting and can save money if your business expenses are low.

Cash Accounting Scheme

You account for VAT when you receive payment from clients, not when you invoice. This means you do not pay VAT on unpaid invoices — which helps cash flow.

Annual Accounting Scheme

You make fixed monthly or quarterly payments to HMRC based on estimated turnover, then reconcile annually. This spreads the cost and makes budgeting easier.

Making Tax Digital (MTD) for VAT

All VAT-registered businesses must keep digital records and submit VAT returns using MTD-compatible software. If you are registered for VAT, you must use software like FreeAgent, Xero, or QuickBooks — you cannot file VAT returns through the old HMRC portal.

Corporation Tax: If You Are a Limited Company

If you operate as a limited company, your company pays Corporation Tax on its profits. You do not pay Income Tax on the company’s profits directly — instead, you pay yourself through a combination of salary and dividends.

Corporation Tax Rates (2024/25)

Profit LevelCorporation Tax Rate
Up to £50,00019%
£50,001 to £250,000Marginal rate (effective 26.5%)
Over £250,00025%

The marginal rate applies to profits between £50,000 and £250,000, creating an effective rate of 26.5% on the profits within that band. This means there is a point where increasing profits actually results in higher tax — at approximately £250,000, the marginal benefit disappears.

Filing Requirements

  • Annual accounts must be filed at Companies House within 9 months of your financial year end
  • Corporation Tax return (CT600) must be filed with HMRC within 12 months
  • Corporation Tax payment is due 9 months and 1 day after your financial year end

Late filing results in automatic penalties: £150 for accounts filed up to 1 month late, rising to £1,500 for accounts filed more than 6 months late.

Dividends

As a director of a limited company, you pay yourself through dividends. Dividends are paid from post-tax profits.

  • First £1,000 of dividends: 0% (dividend allowance)
  • Dividends up to £50,270: 8.75% (basic rate)
  • Dividends £50,271 to £125,140: 33.75% (higher rate)
  • Dividends over £125,140: 39.35% (additional rate)

Saving—£271

At £50,000 turnover, the saving from operating as a limited company is modest — approximately £271 per year. However, as turnover increases, the saving grows significantly. At £80,000+ turnover, limited companies typically save £3,000–£5,000+ per year compared to sole traders.

The trade-off is increased administration: annual accounts, Corporation Tax returns, Companies House filings, and potentially higher accountancy fees (£500–£1,500 per year).

Tips for Self-Employed Success

  1. Separate your bank account from day one. Even if you are a sole trader and not legally required to, a separate business account makes everything easier — bookkeeping, tax returns, and financial clarity.
  2. Track everything from day one. Record every business expense, no matter how small. Use receipt capture apps (most accounting software includes this). Missing legitimate expenses costs you money at tax time.
  3. Invoice promptly. Send invoices immediately after completing work. The longer you delay, the longer you wait to get paid. Use invoicing software to automate reminders.
  4. Chase late payments. You are entitled to statutory interest (8% + Bank of England base rate) on late commercial payments under the Late Payment of Commercial Debts Act 1998. Use it — it incentivises prompt payment.
  5. Consider a limited company if earning over £40,000. Below this threshold, the tax saving is minimal and may not justify the extra admin. Above it, a limited company becomes increasingly tax-efficient.
  6. Get an accountant. A good accountant pays for themselves. They will identify tax savings you miss, ensure you comply with HMRC rules, and handle your Self Assessment or Corporation Tax return. Expect to pay £300–£1,500 per year depending on complexity.
  7. Set aside money for tax. Put 25–30% of every payment into a separate savings account. When your tax bill arrives, the money is already there.
  8. Keep records for 6 years. HMRC can inspect your tax returns going back 6 years. Keep receipts, invoices, bank statements, and expense records organised and accessible.
  9. Review your insurance annually. As your business grows, your insurance needs change. What was sufficient last year may not be enough this year.
  10. Build an emergency fund. Self-employment income is irregular. Keep 6 months’ personal expenses in a separate savings account to cover quiet periods.
