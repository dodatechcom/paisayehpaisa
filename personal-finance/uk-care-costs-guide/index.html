UK Care Costs: How to Plan and Pay for Care

Care costs in the UK are one of the largest financial risks most people face — and one of the least planned for. A care home stay can cost tens of thousands of pounds per year, and without proper planning, your savings and home could be swallowed up. This guide explains how UK care costs work, what help is available, and how to protect your finances.

The Scale of UK Care Costs

The average cost of care in the UK is substantial and rising:

  • Care home (residential): £800 to £1,200 per week (£40,000 to £60,000 per year)
  • Care home (nursing): £1,000 to £1,500 per week (£50,000 to £75,000 per year)
  • Home care: £15 to £25 per hour, depending on location and level of need
  • Live-in care: £800 to £1,500 per week

These costs vary significantly across the UK. London and the South East tend to be most expensive, while the North of England and Wales are generally cheaper.

Why Most People Don’t Plan

Despite the magnitude of these costs, most people do not plan for care:

  • It feels unlikely — people assume it won’t happen to them
  • It’s uncomfortable — thinking about losing independence is distressing
  • The system is confusing — NHS, councils, means tests, and funding rules are complex
  • The trigger is sudden — a fall, stroke, or dementia diagnosis often forces urgent decisions

Planning early gives you more options and more control over where and how you receive care.

NHS Continuing Healthcare (CHC)

NHS Continuing Healthcare is free care funded entirely by the NHS. It is available if you have a “primary health need” — meaning your care needs are primarily medical rather than social.

Eligibility

To qualify for CHC, your needs must meet at least one of the following descriptors:

  • Behaviour that challenges professionals
  • Cognitive and cognitive-based needs
  • Communication
  • Mobility
  • Nutrition
  • Continence
  • Skin integrity
  • Breathing
  • Altered states of consciousness
  • Emotional and psychological needs
  • Medication management
  • Complex health needs

The key test is whether your needs are primarily health-related. If your needs are mainly about personal care (washing, dressing, eating), you are less likely to qualify. If you have complex medical needs such as wound care, medication management, or unpredictable health conditions, you are more likely to qualify.

How to Apply

  • Request an assessment — contact your GP, hospital discharge team, or local Clinical Commissioning Group (CCG)
  • Describe your needs clearly — focus on health-related needs, not just social care
  • Get a checklist — your GP or discharge team should complete a Decision Support Tool (DST)
  • Appeal if refused — many initial decisions are overturned on appeal. You can request an independent review

What CHC Covers

If you qualify, the NHS funds all your care needs — including:

  • Care home fees (including accommodation costs)
  • Home care visits
  • Equipment and adaptations
  • Support for your carers

CHC is not means-tested. Your savings and income do not affect eligibility.

Local Authority Assessment

If you do not qualify for CHC, your local council may contribute to your care costs. Every council in England must assess anyone who appears to need care and support.

The Assessment Process

  1. Request an assessment — contact your local council’s adult social services team
  2. Needs assessment — a social worker visits you to assess your care needs
  3. Financial assessment (means test) — the council assesses your income and savings
  4. Care plan — if eligible, the council creates a care and support plan

The Means Test Thresholds (2024/25)

The means test determines how much the council contributes to your care:

Assets LevelCouncil Contribution
Below £14,250Council pays maximum support
£14,250 to £23,250Council pays partial support (you contribute)
Above £23,250You pay full costs yourself
  • Lower threshold (£14,250): Your income is assessed but not your savings
  • Upper threshold (£23,250): You must fund your own care

What Counts as Assets

The means test includes:

  • Savings and investments (ISAs, bonds, NS&I)
  • Property (if you are moving into a care home)
  • Valuable possessions
  • Income from pensions and investments

What Is Excluded

  • Your partner’s savings (if you jointly own property, only your share is counted)
  • Your home (if you receive care at home)
  • Personal possessions of low value
  • Certain insurance policies and pension plans

Property Disregard

If you are receiving care at home, your property is generally excluded from the means test. If you move into a care home, your property is included — unless:

  • Your partner still lives there
  • A dependent child under 16 lives there
  • You are receiving temporary care (with a planned return home)

Deferred Payment Agreements (DPA)

A Deferred Payment Agreement is a loan from your council that allows you to use your property to fund care without selling it immediately.

How It Works

  • The council places a charge on your property
  • You receive care funded by the council
  • When you die or sell the property, the council is repaid from the proceeds
  • Your family can inherit the remaining equity

Eligibility

To qualify for a DPA:

  • You must be in a care home (not receiving care at home)
  • You must own your own property
  • Your assets (excluding property) must be below the means test upper threshold
  • Your property must be worth enough to cover the council’s costs plus a buffer

Interest and Fees

Councils charge interest on DPAs, typically around 1.5% to 2% per year. There may also be administrative fees. The exact rates vary by council.

Benefits of a DPA

  • You do not need to sell your home to pay for care
  • Your family can inherit the property
  • The debt is only repaid from the estate — you do not owe it personally
  • It gives you time to decide whether to sell

Funding Options for Care

Beyond CHC and council support, there are several ways to fund care:

Personal Savings

Using your own savings is the most straightforward option, but it can deplete your estate quickly. A care home costing £1,000 per week will use £52,000 per year from savings.

Equity Release

Equity release allows you to access the value locked in your home without selling it. Common options include:

  • Lifetime mortgage — borrow against your home, repaid when you die or move into permanent care
  • Home reversion plan — sell a share of your home to a provider in exchange for a lump sum or income

Equity release reduces the value of your estate and may affect your eligibility for means-tested benefits. Take independent financial advice before proceeding.

Care Fee Annuities

A care fee annuity (or care annuity) is a guaranteed income for life, specifically designed to cover care costs. You pay a lump sum upfront and receive a regular income to fund care. The income is guaranteed regardless of how long you need care.

Benefits include:

  • Predictable, guaranteed income
  • No risk of outliving your savings
  • May be inflation-linked

Long-Term Care Insurance

Long-term care insurance pays out if you need care. Policies vary widely, but may cover:

  • Residential care costs
  • Home care costs
  • A fixed weekly amount

Availability is limited and premiums can be high. It is best purchased when you are relatively young and healthy.

Family Contributions

Many families contribute to care costs. This can include:

  • Direct contributions from children’s income or savings
  • Family members providing unpaid care
  • Using a family property to fund care

Be aware of the emotional and financial strain on family members. Clear agreements about contributions should be documented.

Power of Attorney: Essential for Care Planning

A Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) allows you to appoint someone to make decisions on your behalf if you lose mental capacity. There are two types:

Health and Welfare LPA

Covers decisions about:

  • Medical treatment
  • Care home placement
  • Life-sustaining treatment
  • Day-to-day care

Property and Financial Affairs LPA

Covers decisions about:

  • Paying bills and managing bank accounts
  • Buying and selling property
  • Making investment decisions
  • Managing pension income

Why It Matters for Care

Without an LPA, your family may need to apply to the Court of Protection for a Deputyship Order if you lose capacity. This is:

  • Expensive (court fees of £371 or more)
  • Slow (can take months)
  • Stressful for your family
  • Out of your control

An LPA must be registered while you still have mental capacity. Do it before you need it.

How to Set Up an LPA

  1. Choose your attorney — a trusted person (family member, friend, or professional)
  2. Complete the LPA form — available from the Office of the Public Guardian or online
  3. Register the LPA — pay the registration fee (£82 per LPA in England and Wales)
  4. Store the LPA safely — inform your attorney and relevant institutions

Worked Example: Planning for Care

Margaret, 72, widow

Margaret has:

  • £200,000 in savings (ISAs, pension, and bank accounts)
  • A house worth £300,000
  • A state pension of £10,000 per year
  • A small workplace pension of £6,000 per year

Margaret has a stroke and needs residential care. Her local care home costs £1,000 per week (£52,000 per year).

Step 1: NHS CHC Assessment

Margaret’s GP applies for CHC. Her needs include medication management, mobility, and communication difficulties. After assessment, she is found to have a primary health need and qualifies for full NHS CHC funding.

Result: All care costs are paid by the NHS. Margaret keeps her savings and home.

Step 2: If CHC Is Not Granted

If Margaret does not qualify for CHC, the council conducts a financial assessment:

  • Her assets total £500,000 (£200,000 savings + £300,000 property)
  • She is above the £23,250 upper threshold
  • She must fund her own care

Step 3: Deferred Payment Agreement

Margaret applies for a DPA:

  • The council places a charge on her property
  • The council pays the care home directly
  • Margaret contributes her pension income (£16,000 per year) towards care costs
  • The council funds the remaining £36,000 per year
  • After 5 years, the debt is £180,000

Step 4: Family Top-Up

Margaret’s daughter wants her to stay in a preferred care home that costs £1,200 per week. The council pays the standard rate of £1,000 per week. Margaret’s daughter contributes the £200 per week difference.

The Outcome

With planning, Margaret:

  • Keeps her home for her family to inherit
  • Receives the care she needs
  • Avoids selling assets in a rush
  • Has a clear plan for her finances

Without planning, Margaret could:

  • Be forced to sell her home quickly
  • Miss out on CHC eligibility
  • Have less control over where she receives care
  • Leave her family with a complex, stressful situation

Tips for Planning UK Care Costs

  1. Plan early — start thinking about care costs in your 50s or 60s, not when you need care
  2. Get Power of Attorney — set up both health and financial LPA before you need them
  3. Explore CHC eligibility — if you have health needs, apply for CHC. It is free and can save your estate
  4. Understand the means test — know what counts as assets and what is excluded
  5. Consider equity release — if your home is your main asset, equity release can fund care without selling
  6. Review annually — your financial situation and care needs change. Review your plan each year
  7. Get financial advice — a financial adviser specialising in care costs can help you navigate the options
  8. Talk to your family — discuss your wishes and how you want to fund care before a crisis forces decisions
  9. Document your wishes — write down where you want to receive care and who you want to make decisions
  10. Check insurance — some older life insurance or critical illness policies may cover care costs

Final Thoughts

Care costs are one of the biggest financial risks you will face in later life. Without planning, the system can feel overwhelming and the costs can devastate your estate. With early planning — CHC assessment, means test understanding, DPAs, and Power of Attorney — you can protect your wealth and ensure you receive the care you need. Start planning today, not when a crisis forces your hand.
