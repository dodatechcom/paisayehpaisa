UK Electric Car Salary Sacrifice: Drive Electric, Save Tax

June 16, 2026
🏷️ electric-cars 🏷️ salary-sacrifice 🏷️ bik-tax 🏷️ tax-saving 🏷️ ev 🏷️ workplace-benefits

Electric car salary sacrifice is one of the most generous tax-saving opportunities available to UK employees. By giving up part of your salary in exchange for an electric car, you can drive a brand-new EV with insurance, maintenance, and road tax included — all while paying minimal tax. The savings can be thousands of pounds compared to buying or leasing a car privately.

What Is Electric Car Salary Sacrifice?

Electric car salary sacrifice is a scheme where your employer provides you with an electric car in exchange for a portion of your gross salary. Because the sacrifice is from your pre-tax salary, you pay less income tax and National Insurance. The car is provided on a lease, so it includes insurance, maintenance, and road tax as part of the package.

How It Works

  1. You choose an electric car from the scheme’s approved list
  2. Your employer arranges the lease through a salary sacrifice provider
  3. You agree a monthly salary sacrifice amount
  4. Your gross salary is reduced by that amount
  5. You receive the car, plus insurance, maintenance, and road tax
  6. You pay Benefit in Kind (BIK) tax on the car — but for electric cars, this is very low

The key advantage is the low BIK tax rate. While a petrol car might have a BIK rate of 20-37%, electric cars are taxed at just 2% for 2024/25 and 2025/26. This makes the total cost of driving an electric car through salary sacrifice significantly cheaper than any other way of getting a car.

BIK Tax: Why Electric Cars Win

Benefit in Kind (BIK) tax is the tax you pay on company car benefits. It is calculated as a percentage of the car’s list price, and that percentage depends on the car’s CO2 emissions.

BIK Rates Comparison

Car TypeCO2 EmissionsBIK Rate (2024/25)Annual BIK Tax on £40,000 Car
Electric car0 g/km2%£160
Plug-in hybrid (low emission)1-50 g/km5-12%£400-£960
Petrol car (average)120 g/km30%£2,400
Diesel car (average)140 g/km31%£2,480
High-emission petrol180+ g/km37%£2,960

The difference is stark. A £40,000 electric car costs just £160 per year in BIK tax. The same car as a petrol vehicle would cost £2,400-2,960 per year. That is a saving of over £2,000 per year in BIK tax alone.

How BIK Tax Is Calculated

BIK tax is calculated as follows:

  • BIK value = List price x BIK rate
  • Annual BIK tax = BIK value x your income tax rate

For a £40,000 electric car at 2% BIK:

  • BIK value: £40,000 x 2% = £800
  • Annual BIK tax (40% taxpayer): £800 x 40% = £320
  • Monthly BIK tax: £26.67

For a £40,000 petrol car at 30% BIK:

  • BIK value: £40,000 x 30% = £12,000
  • Annual BIK tax (40% taxpayer): £12,000 x 40% = £4,800
  • Monthly BIK tax: £400

The electric car saves you £373 per month in BIK tax alone.

How Salary Sacrifice Saves on Electric Cars

When you use salary sacrifice for an electric car, there are two layers of savings:

1. Income Tax and NI Savings on the Sacrifice

Your gross salary is reduced by the sacrifice amount, so you pay less income tax and National Insurance. For a higher rate taxpayer sacrificing £400 per month:

  • Income tax saved: £400 x 40% = £160
  • Employee NI saved: £400 x 2% = £8
  • Total monthly saving: £168

2. Low BIK Tax

Instead of paying income tax on the full sacrifice amount, you pay just 2% BIK tax on the car’s list price. This is far less than the income tax you would pay on the same amount of cash salary.

Combined Savings

The combination of salary sacrifice savings and low BIK tax makes electric car salary sacrifice one of the most tax-efficient ways to get a car. The total cost to you (after tax savings) is often significantly less than leasing or buying privately.

Charging Costs: Home vs Public

One of the running costs of an electric car is charging. The cost varies significantly depending on where you charge:

Home charging is by far the cheapest option. If you can charge at home overnight on an off-peak tariff (7p/kWh), a 60kWh battery costs just £4.20 to charge fully — enough for around 200 miles of driving. That works out at roughly 1.8p per mile.

By comparison, a petrol car doing 40 miles per gallon at £1.45 per litre costs around 16p per mile. Electric charging at home is approximately 9 times cheaper per mile.

Installing a Home Charger

A home wallbox charger typically costs £800-1,200 to install, including the unit and fitting. The government previously offered a Plug-In Grant of £350 towards installation, though this has been reduced in recent years. Check the latest grants on GOV.UK.

Even without a grant, the savings on charging costs mean a home charger pays for itself quickly. If you drive 10,000 miles per year, home charging at 1.8p per mile costs £180. Public charging at 10p per mile would cost £1,000 — a difference of £820 per year.

What You Get for £208 Per Month

For that net monthly cost, you get:

  • A brand-new electric car worth £40,000
  • Comprehensive insurance
  • All maintenance and servicing
  • Road tax (VED) included
  • Breakdown cover (on most schemes)
  • Replacement tyres

Try getting all of that for £208 per month on the private market. It is virtually impossible.

Tips for Getting the Most from Electric Car Salary Sacrifice

Check If Your Employer Offers the Scheme

Not all employers offer electric car salary sacrifice, but the number is growing rapidly. Contact your HR or payroll department to find out. If they do not currently offer it, providers like Octopus Electric Vehicles, salary sacrifice specialists, and fleet management companies can help set it up.

Choose a Car with Low BIK

The BIK rate applies to the car’s list price, so a cheaper car means lower BIK tax. However, the salary sacrifice savings are based on the monthly cost, so there is a balance to strike. Popular choices include the MG4, BYD Atto 3, and Cupra Born — all offering good value with low BIK.

Install a Home Charger

Home charging is the cheapest way to run an electric car. If you have off-street parking, install a wallbox charger. The upfront cost is typically recovered within a year through charging savings.

Consider Total Cost of Ownership

When comparing electric car salary sacrifice with other options, look at the total cost of ownership, not just the monthly payment:

  • Fuel/charging costs — Electric is much cheaper than petrol or diesel
  • Insurance — Often included in salary sacrifice, but check
  • Maintenance — Electric cars have fewer moving parts, so servicing is cheaper
  • Road tax — Electric cars are exempt from VED (until 2025 when the rules change)
  • Depreciation — Electric cars are holding value better than expected

Compare with PCP and HP

Before committing to salary sacrifice, compare with other finance options:

Check the Scheme Terms

Before signing up, check:

  • Minimum lease period — Typically 2-4 years
  • Mileage allowance — Most schemes include 10,000-15,000 miles per year. Extra miles may incur charges
  • End of lease options — You typically return the car, but some schemes allow you to buy it
  • Early termination — What happens if you leave your job or want to exit the scheme

Factor In Your Commute

Electric car salary sacrifice works best if you commute regularly. If you only drive occasionally, the savings may not justify the commitment. Consider how many miles you drive and whether an electric car suits your journey patterns.

Pros and Cons

Pros

  • Huge tax savings — Save thousands compared to buying or leasing privately
  • Low BIK tax — Just 2% for electric cars vs 20-37% for petrol
  • All-inclusive package — Insurance, maintenance, and road tax included
  • No upfront cost — The car is provided through the lease
  • Environmental benefits — Zero tailpipe emissions
  • Home charging is cheap — As little as 1.8p per mile

Cons

  • Reduced gross salary — This can affect mortgage affordability
  • Commitment period — Typically 2-4 years
  • Must return the car — At the end of the lease (unless you buy it separately)
  • Mileage limits — Exceeding your allowance incurs extra charges
  • Not all employers offer it — You need your employer to participate
  • Charging infrastructure — Home charging requires off-street parking

Who Benefits Most

Ideal Candidates

  • Higher rate taxpayers (40%+) — The tax saving is greatest at higher rates
  • Regular commuters — The more you drive, the more you save on fuel too
  • People with off-street parking — Home charging maximises the savings
  • Those wanting a new car — Salary sacrifice provides a brand-new car with no deposit
  • Environmentally conscious drivers — Zero emissions from the tailpipe

Less Suitable For

  • Low earners — Below the NI threshold, the saving is smaller
  • People without home charging — Public charging is more expensive
  • Those planning to leave their job — Early termination can be costly
  • Occasional drivers — The savings may not justify the commitment
  • People needing large cars — Electric SUVs and larger cars have higher list prices and lease costs
