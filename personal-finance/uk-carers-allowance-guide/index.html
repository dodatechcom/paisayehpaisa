UK Carer's Allowance: Financial Support for Unpaid Carers

June 16, 2026
🏷️ carers-allowance 🏷️ carers-credit 🏷️ carers-premium 🏷️ disability 🏷️ dwp 🏷️ universal-credit 🏷️ national-insurance 🏷️ unpaid-carers

Across the UK, millions of people provide unpaid care to a family member, friend, or neighbour with a disability, illness, or age-related condition. If this sounds like you, you may be entitled to Carer’s Allowance — a weekly payment from the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) designed to help with the costs of caring. This guide covers everything you need to know, from eligibility to claiming and how it interacts with other benefits.

What Is Carer’s Allowance?

Carer’s Allowance is a weekly payment of £81.90 (2024/25) for people who spend at least 35 hours per week caring for someone who receives certain disability benefits. It is one of the lowest benefits of its kind, but it comes with valuable extras — including National Insurance credits and access to the Carer’s Premium.

You do not need to live with the person you care for, and the care you provide does not need to be full-time nursing. What matters is the time you spend helping with their physical or mental health needs, or supervising to keep them safe.

Eligibility

To qualify for Carer’s Allowance, you must meet all of the following criteria:

  • You spend 35 or more hours per week caring for someone
  • The person you care for receives one of these benefits: Personal Independence Payment (PIP) daily living component, Disability Living Allowance (DLA) at the middle or highest rate, or Attendance Allowance
  • You are 16 or over and not in full-time education (21 hours or more per week)
  • You earn £151 or less per week after tax, National Insurance, and pension contributions
  • You are not receiving certain other benefits that prevent you from claiming (such as New Style Employment and Support Allowance at the full rate)

The earnings limit applies to you, not the person you care for. Their income and savings are not relevant.

How to Claim

You can claim Carer’s Allowance in two ways:

  1. Online at gov.uk/carers-allowance
  2. By phone — call the DWP Carer’s Allowance Unit on 0800 731 0297

You will need:

  • Your National Insurance number
  • Bank or building society details for payments
  • The name, address, and date of birth of the person you care for
  • Their National Insurance number (if they have one)
  • Details of the disability benefit they receive
  • Your GP’s name and address
  • Your employment details, if you work

Claims can be backdated by up to three months, so do not delay if you think you may be eligible.

Impact on Other Benefits

Carer’s Allowance is treated as income for means-tested benefits. This means it can affect:

  • Universal Credit — Carer’s Allowance reduces your Universal Credit payment pound for pound. However, you may also receive the carer element in Universal Credit (£198.31/month in 2024/25), which can offset this
  • Housing Benefit — counted as income in the means test
  • Council Tax Reduction — counted as income by your local council

The good news is that receiving Carer’s Allowance gives you National Insurance credits for each week you receive it. These credits count towards your State Pension entitlement, which is especially valuable if you are not working enough to build up NI credits through employment.

Carer’s Credit

If you do not qualify for Carer’s Allowance — for example, because you earn more than £151 per week or the person you care for does not receive a qualifying benefit — you may still be able to get Carer’s Credit.

Carer’s Credit provides National Insurance credits without any means test. You can claim it if you are caring for someone for 20 hours or more per week and they receive (or are waiting to receive) a disability benefit. You do not need to be caring for 35 hours per week, and there is no earnings limit.

To apply, contact the Carer’s Allowance Unit or use form CR1.

Carer’s Premium

The Carer’s Premium is an extra amount of money paid on top of certain means-tested benefits. It is £42.75 per week (2024/25).

You may receive the Carer’s Premium if you:

  • Are receiving Carer’s Allowance, or
  • Have been receiving Carer’s Allowance within the last 8 weeks, or
  • Have been receiving Carer’s Allowance and it stopped because the person you care for moved into a care home or hospital

The Carer’s Premium can be included in the means test for:

  • Pension Credit
  • Housing Benefit
  • Council Tax Reduction
  • Income-based Jobseeker’s Allowance
  • Income-related Employment and Support Allowance

Worked Example: Emma

Emma is 45 and cares for her mother, who is 82 and receives Attendance Allowance. Emma works part-time, earning £140 per week after tax and National Insurance.

Emma’s eligibility:

  • She cares for her mother for 36 hours per week (meets the 35-hour requirement)
  • Her mother receives Attendance Allowance (qualifying benefit)
  • Emma earns £140 per week, which is under the £151 limit

Emma’s Carer’s Allowance calculation:

ItemAmount
Part-time earnings£140.00
Carer’s Allowance£81.90
Total weekly income£221.90

Emma also receives National Insurance credits, which protect her State Pension entitlement for each week she receives Carer’s Allowance.

Tips for Unpaid Carers

  • Check your eligibility even if you are not sure you qualify — many carers miss out because they assume their earnings are too high or their situation does not count
  • Apply even if you think you will be turned down — the application process can unlock other support and National Insurance credits
  • Request a Carer’s Assessment from your local council. This is a free assessment of your needs as a carer and can lead to services such as respite care, help with transport, or direct payments
  • Join Carers UK (carersuk.org) for free advice, a helpline (0808 808 7777), and support from other carers
  • Keep records of the hours you spend caring — this will help with your application and any future assessments
  • Tell the DWP if your circumstances change, such as if you start earning more or the person you care for stops receiving their disability benefit
