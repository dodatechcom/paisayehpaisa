Universal Credit Explained: Complete Guide

June 16, 2026
🏷️ universal-credit 🏷️ benefits 🏷️ welfare 🏷️ working-tax-credit 🏷️ housing-benefit 🏷️ jobseekers-allowance 🏷️ dwp

Universal Credit is the UK Government’s main means-tested benefit for people of working age on a low income. It replaced six legacy benefits with a single monthly payment, designed to make the system simpler and ensure that work always pays.

What Universal Credit Replaced

Universal Credit rolled up six previous benefits into one:

  • Working Tax Credit — for people on low incomes who are working
  • Child Tax Credit — for families with children on low incomes
  • Housing Benefit — help with rent for people on low incomes
  • Jobseeker’s Allowance (income-based) — for people actively looking for work
  • Employment and Support Allowance (income-related) — for people who cannot work due to illness or disability
  • Income Support — for people on very low incomes who are not required to seek work

If you still receive any of these legacy benefits, you may be moved to Universal Credit through managed migration — the government’s process of transferring remaining claimants.

Standard Allowance Rates (2026/27)

The standard allowance is the basic amount you receive before any additional elements (such as housing, children, or disability). The monthly rates for 2026/27 are:

Claimant situationMonthly amount
Single, under 25£311.68
Single, 25 or over£393.45
Joint claimants, both under 25£489.23
Joint claimants, one 25 or over£617.56

On top of the standard allowance, you may receive additional amounts for:

  • Housing costs — rent (subject to Local Housing Allowance caps)
  • Children — £315.00 per month for the first child, £270.00 for each subsequent child (2026/27 rates)
  • Disability — limited capability for work element or limited capability for work and work-related activity element
  • Carer — if you provide care for 35+ hours per week

How the Taper Rate Works

Universal Credit is designed so that you are always better off working. As your earnings increase, your Universal Credit payment reduces — but not pound for pound.

The taper rate is 55p for every £1 earned above your work allowance. This means for every extra £1 you earn beyond the threshold, your Universal Credit drops by 55p, leaving you with 45p.

Work Allowance

Before the taper rate kicks in, you can earn a certain amount without any reduction. This is called the work allowance:

  • £404 per month — if you do not receive help with housing costs
  • £263 per month — if you do receive help with housing costs

Example Calculation

Say you are single, aged 28, renting, and receive housing cost support:

  • Standard allowance: £393.45
  • Housing costs element: £450.00
  • Total maximum UC: £843.45

You start working and earn £1,200 per month. Your work allowance (with housing costs) is £263.

  • Earnings above work allowance: £1,200 − £263 = £937
  • Taper deduction: £937 × 0.55 = £515.35
  • Your Universal Credit: £843.45 − £515.35 = £328.10

So you keep your earnings plus £328.10 from Universal Credit — a total of £1,528.10, compared to £843.45 with no work.

The 5 Week Wait

When you first claim Universal Credit, there is typically a 5-week waiting period before your first payment. This is one of the most significant challenges for new claimants.

During this period you can:

  • Request an advance payment (a loan that is repaid from future UC payments)
  • Apply for a Discretionary Housing Payment if you are at risk of losing your home
  • Contact your local council for emergency support

Advance Payments

If you cannot manage during the 5-week wait, you can ask for an advance. Key points:

  • Advances are loans — they must be repaid
  • Repayment is deducted from your future Universal Credit payments (up to 25% per month)
  • You can request up to 100% of your estimated monthly entitlement
  • Repayment can be spread over up to 24 months in exceptional circumstances

Managed Migration

The government is moving people on legacy benefits to Universal Credit through managed migration. If you are affected:

  • You will receive a Migration Notice giving you at least 3 months to claim
  • Your existing benefits will stop automatically on a set date
  • You must make a new Universal Credit claim
  • Transitional protection may apply — this tops up your Universal Credit to match what you were receiving under legacy benefits, and stays in place until your circumstances change

Do not ignore a Migration Notice. If you miss the deadline, you could lose transitional protection.

How to Apply

You apply for Universal Credit online at gov.uk/universal-credit. Before starting your application, gather:

  • National Insurance number
  • Bank or building society details — for payment
  • Housing costs information — rent statement, tenancy agreement
  • Details of any savings or investments — over £6,000 affects your payment
  • Information about your earnings — payslips, employer details
  • Childcare costs — if you want to claim childcare element
  • Health information — if claiming disability elements (you may need a Fit Note from your GP)

If You Cannot Apply Online

You can call the Universal Credit helpline on 0800 328 5644 to claim by phone. You can also get help with your claim at your local Jobcentre Plus or from Citizens Advice.

What Happens After You Claim

Once your claim is submitted:

  1. You will be assigned a Work Coach at your local Jobcentre Plus
  2. You will have a commitment — a set of actions you must agree to (looking for work, increasing hours, etc.)
  3. You must report any change of circumstances — earnings, rent changes, household changes — through your online journal
  4. Your payment is calculated monthly in arrears and paid directly into your bank account

Tips for Managing on Universal Credit

  • Budget monthly — UC is paid once a month, not weekly. Set aside money for rent, bills, and food
  • Use the UC advance wisely — it is a loan, not extra money. Only borrow what you need
  • Report changes promptly — underpayments or overpayments can cause problems
  • Keep a journal — your UC journal is your record of communication with the DWP. Check it regularly
  • Get free advice — Citizens Advice, Turn2Us, and StepChange all offer free guidance on Universal Credit

Key Takeaways

  • Universal Credit replaces six legacy benefits with one monthly payment
  • The 2026/27 standard allowance ranges from £311.68 to £617.56 per month depending on your age and claim type
  • The 55% taper rate means you always keep more when you earn more
  • There is a 5-week wait for your first payment — plan ahead or request an advance
  • Apply online at gov.uk/universal-credit or call 0800 328 5644
