UK Cycle to Work Scheme: Get a Tax-Free Bike

June 16, 2026
🏷️ cycle-to-work 🏷️ salary-sacrifice 🏷️ tax-saving 🏷️ commuting 🏷️ bicycle 🏷️ workplace-benefits

The Cycle to Work scheme is one of the simplest and most generous tax-free benefits available to UK employees. It lets you get a bicycle and accessories through your employer, paying nothing upfront and saving 28-42% on the total cost. If you commute by bike — or want to start — this scheme is well worth understanding.

What Is the Cycle to Work Scheme?

The Cycle to Work scheme is a government-backed tax incentive that lets employers provide bicycles and cycling equipment to employees as a tax-free benefit. You agree to give up part of your salary (salary sacrifice) over a set period, and in return you get a bike and accessories without paying income tax or National Insurance on the value.

How It Works

  1. You choose a bike and accessories from a participating retailer or through your employer’s scheme provider
  2. Your employer buys the bike on your behalf
  3. You repay the cost through salary sacrifice over 12-18 months (scheme dependent)
  4. Because the sacrifice is from gross salary, you pay no income tax or National Insurance on the amount
  5. At the end of the hire period, you can purchase the bike at a small residual value

The key advantage is that you are effectively buying the bike at a discount of 28-42%, depending on your tax rate, because you never pay income tax or National Insurance on the salary you sacrifice.

How Much Can You Save?

The amount you save depends on your income tax rate and National Insurance contributions. Here is the breakdown:

Tax RateIncome Tax SavedNI SavedTotal Saving Per £1
Basic rate (20%)£0.20£0.08£0.28
Higher rate (40%)£0.40£0.02£0.42
Additional rate (45%)£0.45£0.02£0.47

Higher and additional rate taxpayers pay only 2% National Insurance above the upper earnings limit, so their NI saving is smaller. However, the income tax saving more than compensates, making the total saving significantly higher for higher earners.

Scheme Limits

Different Cycle to Work scheme providers set different maximum limits on the value of bikes and equipment you can purchase:

  • Government-backed scheme: £3,000 limit (this is the most common cap)
  • Some private schemes: No upper limit — you can spend more, though very expensive bikes may attract benefit in kind tax
  • Employer-specific schemes: Varies by employer — some set their own limits based on budget

If you want a bike worth more than £3,000, check whether your employer uses a scheme without a cap. Some providers allow higher spending, though you should be aware that anything above £3,000 may be treated as a benefit in kind and could be subject to tax.

What’s Included?

The scheme covers more than just the bicycle itself. You can typically include:

  • Bicycle — Any type: road bike, mountain bike, hybrid, folding bike, or cargo bike
  • Helmet — Safety helmets are an eligible expense
  • Lights — Front and rear lights for safety
  • Lock — D-locks, chain locks, or cable locks
  • Clothing — Hi-vis jackets, waterproofs, and cycling-specific clothing (some schemes)
  • Bag or panniers — Cycling bags that attach to your bike
  • Maintenance kit — Pumps, puncture repair kits, and basic tools

E-Bikes

Many schemes now include electric bikes (e-bikes). E-bikes are particularly useful for longer commutes or hilly routes. The same tax savings apply — you can save 28-42% on an e-bike through the scheme. If your commute is more than 5-10 miles each way, an e-bike can make the journey far more manageable.

End of Scheme: Buying the Bike

At the end of the hire period, you have the option to purchase the bike from your employer. This is typically done at a “fair market value” (FMV), which reflects the bike’s depreciation.

Typical Residual Values

The amount you pay depends on how long you have had the bike and its original value. HMRC provides guidance on typical residual values:

Original ValueAfter 1 YearAfter 3 YearsAfter 5 Years
£500£38 (7.6%)£25 (5%)£13 (2.6%)
£1,000£76 (7.6%)£50 (5%)£25 (2.5%)
£2,000£152 (7.6%)£100 (5%)£50 (2.5%)
£3,000£228 (7.6%)£150 (5%)£75 (2.5%)

The residual value is typically 3-7% of the original price after 1-3 years. This is a small amount compared to the tax savings you have already received.

What Happens If You Don’t Buy?

If you do not want to buy the bike, you simply return it to your employer at the end of the hire period. You have still benefited from the tax-free salary sacrifice during the hire period, so you have still saved money.

Worked Example: £500 Bike

Let’s walk through a complete example to show exactly how the savings work.

Scenario

  • Bike cost: £500
  • Taxpayer status: Higher rate (40%)
  • Salary sacrifice period: 12 months
  • Monthly sacrifice: £41.67

Tax Savings During Hire Period

ItemAmount
Bike cost£500
Income tax saved (40%)£200
Employee NI saved (2%)£10

Net cost of bike after tax savings£290

End of Scheme Purchase

ItemAmount
Fair market value (after 1 year)£38
Total cost to you£328

For a higher rate taxpayer, a £500 bike ends up costing just £328 — a saving of £172, or 34%. You get the bike, a helmet, lights, and a lock, all at a significant discount.

Tips for Getting the Most from Cycle to Work

Check If Your Employer Offers the Scheme

Not all employers offer Cycle to Work, but many do. Ask your HR or payroll department. If they do not currently offer it, it is relatively simple for them to set up through a scheme provider such as Cyclescheme, Bike2Work, or Green Commute Initiative.

Include Accessories

Do not just buy the bike. Add a helmet, lights, a lock, and any other cycling equipment you need. These are all covered by the scheme and you save the same tax on them. A good set of accessories can easily add £100-200 to your order.

Consider an E-Bike for Longer Commutes

If your commute is more than 5 miles each way, or you have hills to contend with, an e-bike can make the journey far more pleasant. The same tax savings apply, and many schemes include e-bikes. An e-bike typically costs £1,000-3,000, making the tax savings even more significant.

Budget for the End-of-Scheme Purchase

When you sign up for the scheme, remember that you will need to pay a small amount at the end to buy the bike. This is usually £25-150 depending on the bike’s value and how long you have had it. Set this money aside so it does not come as a surprise.

Compare with Buying Outright

Before committing to the scheme, compare the total cost with buying the bike outright:

  • Outright purchase: £500 (no tax saving)
  • Cycle to Work (higher rate): £328 total (including end-of-scheme purchase)
  • Cycle to Work (basic rate): £398 total

The scheme wins in almost every scenario, especially for higher rate taxpayers.

Use It Multiple Times

You can use the Cycle to Work scheme more than once. Some schemes have a waiting period between uses (typically 12 months), but there is nothing stopping you from getting a new bike every few years.

Check Scheme Deadlines

Most schemes have enrollment deadlines, often aligned with the tax year or your employer’s payroll cycle. Make sure you sign up in time to benefit.

Pros and Cons

Pros

  • Significant tax savings — 28-42% off the cost of a bike and accessories
  • No upfront cost — The bike is provided by your employer, you pay via salary sacrifice
  • Covers more than bikes — Helmets, lights, locks, clothing, and e-bikes
  • Good for health and the environment — Cycling improves fitness and reduces emissions
  • End-of-scheme purchase is cheap — Typically 3-7% of original price

Cons

  • Must be used for commuting — The bike should be used mainly for travelling to and from work
  • Salary sacrifice reduces gross salary — This can affect mortgage affordability
  • Not all employers offer it — You need your employer to participate
  • End-of-scheme purchase is an extra cost — Small, but worth budgeting for
  • Limited to scheme provider retailers — You may not be able to use your preferred bike shop

Who Benefits Most

Ideal Candidates

  • Regular commuters — If you cycle to work at least 3 days a week, the savings add up quickly
  • Higher rate taxpayers — The 42% saving makes this particularly valuable
  • People buying expensive bikes — The higher the bike value, the greater the absolute saving
  • E-bike buyers — E-bikes are expensive, so the tax saving is more meaningful

Less Suitable For

  • Occasional cyclists — If you rarely commute by bike, the saving may not be worth the commitment
  • People planning to leave their job — You may need to repay the remaining sacrifice
  • Those on very low incomes — Below the NI threshold, the saving is smaller
