UK Social Fund & Crisis Support: Emergency Money When You Need It Most

June 16, 2026
🏷️ social-fund 🏷️ budgeting-loans 🏷️ crisis-support 🏷️ emergency-funding 🏷️ local-welfare 🏷️ food-banks 🏷️ energy-grants 🏷️ universal-credit 🏷️ financial-help

When money runs out before payday, or an emergency hits — a broken boiler, a flooded kitchen, or a sudden job loss — there are safety nets in the UK designed to catch you. Many people never use them simply because they do not know they exist. This guide walks through every major emergency support scheme available to UK residents, with practical steps to apply.

Budgeting Loans

Budgeting Loans are interest-free loans from the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) for people on certain benefits. They are designed to help with essential items you cannot afford from your regular income — things like furniture, clothing, travel costs, or essential household equipment.

Who Can Apply

You must have been receiving one of the following for at least six months:

  • Universal Credit
  • Income Support
  • Pension Credit
  • Income-based Jobseeker’s Allowance (JSA)
  • Income-based Employment and Support Allowance (ESA)

How Much You Can Get

Claimant situationMaximum amount
Single, no children£348
Couple, no children£464
Lone parent£812

The exact amount depends on what you need and your ability to repay. Loans are repaid automatically through deductions from your benefits, typically over 104 weeks.

How to Apply

Apply online through your GOV.UK account or call the DWP Budgeting Loans line on 0800 032 7773. You will need to explain what you need the money for and provide details of your financial situation.

Crisis Loans

Crisis Loans used to be available directly from the DWP for immediate emergencies such as fires, floods, or domestic violence. They have been largely replaced by Local Welfare Assistance Schemes, which are now run by local councils rather than the DWP.

If you are in an immediate emergency, contact your local council. They can provide emergency help through their local scheme or signpost you to other support.

Local Welfare Assistance Schemes

Every council in England, Scotland, and Wales runs its own Local Welfare Assistance Scheme. These schemes provide emergency help with essential items when you have no other means of paying.

What They Cover

  • Essential household items (beds, cookers, fridges)
  • Food and fuel costs
  • Emergency clothing
  • Items needed after a flood, fire, or burglary

How to Apply

Contact your local council directly. Search for your council on GOV.UK and call their housing or welfare team. Each council has its own rules about what they will fund and eligibility criteria, so the process varies by area.

You do not need to be on benefits to apply, but you will usually need to show you cannot afford the item from your own resources.

Discretionary Housing Payments (DHP)

Discretionary Housing Payments are extra money from your council if your Housing Benefit or the housing element of Universal Credit does not cover your full rent. They are designed to help with rent shortfalls, deposits, or moving costs.

What DHP Can Help With

  • The gap between your Housing Benefit and your actual rent
  • Rent in advance if you are moving to a new property
  • Removal costs if you need to move

How to Apply

Apply to your local council. You will need to explain why your current housing benefit is not enough. DHPs are usually temporary, but they can be extended if your circumstances remain difficult. Not all councils have unlimited funds for DHPs, so apply early.

Trussell Trust and Food Banks

The Trussell Trust is the UK’s largest network of food banks, with over 1,200 locations across the country. Food banks provide emergency food parcels when you cannot afford to buy food.

How It Works

Food banks operate on a voucher-based referral system. You cannot simply walk in and ask for food — you need a referral from:

  • Your GP or health visitor
  • Citizens Advice
  • A school or college
  • A social worker
  • Your local council

Once referred, you will receive a three-day emergency food parcel containing enough food for meals during that period. Many food banks also provide additional support, such as toiletries, nappies, and advice on other benefits you may be entitled to.

Finding Your Nearest Food Bank

Visit the Trussell Trust website to find your nearest location and the referral routes available in your area.

Warm Home Discount

The Warm Home Discount is a government scheme that gives you £150 off your electricity bill during winter. It is available to:

  • People receiving Pension Credit
  • Households on a low income who meet their energy supplier’s criteria

The discount is usually applied automatically by your energy supplier between October and March. Check with your provider to confirm you qualify. If you are on a prepayment meter, you may receive a voucher instead.

British Gas Energy Trust

The British Gas Energy Trust provides grants to help clear energy debts. You do not need to be a British Gas customer to apply — the trust is open to everyone.

What the Trust Covers

  • Debts owed to any energy supplier
  • Energy-related debts (including water bills in some cases)

How to Apply

Apply online through the British Gas Energy Trust website. You will need to provide evidence of your financial situation and the debt you want cleared. The trust also provides grants for energy-efficient appliances and home improvements.

Turn2Us Grant Search

Turn2Us is a charity that helps people find financial support. Their Grant Search tool is a free database of charitable grants from thousands of UK charities.

How to Use It

Visit the Turn2Us website and use the Grant Search tool to look for grants based on your:

  • Age
  • Location
  • Financial situation
  • Specific needs (disability, caring responsibilities, health conditions)

Many grants are unclaimed because people do not know they exist. The search is free and confidential, and you can apply directly to the charities listed.

Worked Example: Single Parent on Universal Credit

Here is a practical example of how these schemes work together when an emergency hits.

Situation: Maria is a single parent on Universal Credit. Her washing machine breaks down, and she cannot afford a replacement. She also cannot afford food for the week.

Step 1: Budgeting Loan Maria applies for a Budgeting Loan from the DWP. She is approved for £300 to buy a replacement washing machine. The loan is repaid at £12 per month from her Universal Credit over 25 weeks.

Step 2: Local Welfare Assistance Maria contacts her local council and receives a £100 emergency voucher for food and energy costs while she waits for the Budgeting Loan to be processed.

Step 3: Food Bank Maria gets a referral from her GP for a local food bank. She receives a three-day emergency food parcel to cover her immediately while the loan and council support are arranged.

Total support received: £430

  • £300 Budgeting Loan (repaid at £12/month)
  • £100 council emergency voucher (non-repayable)
  • £30 value food bank parcel (non-repayable)

This is the system working as it should — short-term help to get through a crisis without falling into debt.

Tips for Getting Emergency Support

Don’t Suffer in Silence

Many people feel embarrassed about asking for help. These schemes exist for exactly this purpose. Using them is not a sign of failure — it is using the support your taxes have funded.

Check All Options

You may qualify for several types of support at once. Budgeting Loans, council welfare schemes, food banks, and energy grants can all be used together.

Apply for Budgeting Loans First

Budgeting Loans are interest-free and repaid slowly from benefits. They should be your first port of contact for essential items. The application takes a few minutes online or by phone.

Contact Your Council

Your local council can help with food, fuel, furniture, and housing costs. Even if you are not sure what you qualify for, call and ask. They can also signpost you to other local charities and support.

Use Food Banks Without Shame

Food banks are a vital safety net. Getting a referral from your GP, Citizens Advice, or a school is straightforward. The staff and volunteers are there to help, not to judge.

Get Citizens Advice

Citizens Advice can help you check what benefits you are entitled to, complete applications, and deal with debt. Their service is free, confidential, and available in person, by phone, and online.
